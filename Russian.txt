<p id=""mwDw""><b id=""mwEA"">Russian</b> (русский) is a Slavic language spoken by 300+ million people world-wide. Most people living in <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Russia"" title=""Russia"" id=""mwEQ"">Russia</a> use it as a first language, and many people in <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Central_Asia"" title=""Central Asia"" id=""mwEg"">Central Asia</a>, the <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Caucasus"" title=""Caucasus"" id=""mwEw"">Caucasus</a>, and <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Europe"" title=""Europe"" id=""mwFA"">Eastern Europe</a> know it as a second language. It holds official status in Russia, <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Belarus"" title=""Belarus"" id=""mwFQ"">Belarus</a>, <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Kazakhstan"" title=""Kazakhstan"" id=""mwFg"">Kazakhstan</a>, <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Kyrgyzstan"" title=""Kyrgyzstan"" id=""mwFw"">Kyrgyzstan</a>, and the unrecognized <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Abkhazia"" title=""Abkhazia"" id=""mwGA"">Abkhazia</a>, <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Transnistria"" title=""Transnistria"" id=""mwGQ"">Transnistria</a> and <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/South_Ossetia"" title=""South Ossetia"" id=""mwGg"">South Ossetia</a>.</p>

<p id=""mwGw"">In countries lacking official designation for Russian, such as <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Ukraine"" title=""Ukraine"" id=""mwHA"">Ukraine</a>, <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Georgia_(country)"" title=""Georgia (country)"" id=""mwHQ"">Georgia</a> and the <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Baltic_States"" title=""Baltic States"" class=""mw-redirect"" id=""mwHg"">Baltic States</a> (where schooling in Russian was mandatory under the Soviet era), a solid majority of residents may speak it as a second language, and there are significant native-speaker minorities. However, due to its association with Soviet oppression, many residents in these countries may find it offensive to be addressed in Russian, and younger people will often prefer to speak English than Russian when communicating with foreigners.</p>

<p id=""mwHw"">Likewise, a similar dynamic exists in many of the formerly communist countries of Europe such as <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Poland"" title=""Poland"" id=""mwIA"">Poland</a>, the <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Czech_Republic"" title=""Czech Republic"" id=""mwIQ"">Czech Republic</a> and <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Hungary"" title=""Hungary"" id=""mwIg"">Hungary</a>, in which Russian was a compulsory second language in schools under communist rule.</p>

<p id=""mwIw"">Russian remains the <i id=""mwJA"">lingua franca</i> of choice throughout the former Soviet <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Central_Asia"" title=""Central Asia"" id=""mwJQ"">Central Asian</a> states, <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Armenia"" title=""Armenia"" id=""mwJg"">Armenia</a>, <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Azerbaijan"" title=""Azerbaijan"" id=""mwJw"">Azerbaijan</a> and <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Moldova"" title=""Moldova"" id=""mwKA"">Moldova</a>, where it is effectively the language of commerce, government, and travel (despite lacking official status).</p>

<p id=""mwKQ"">It is, to a lesser extent, an important language in <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Mongolia"" title=""Mongolia"" id=""mwKg"">Mongolia</a>, where it is a compulsory second language in schools, and is the most widely spoken foreign language. Russian has also become the third most widely spoken language in <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Israel"" title=""Israel"" id=""mwKw"">Israel</a>, owing to a massive exodus of Jews from Russia and other ex-Soviet countries in the late 20th and early 21st centuries.</p>

<p id=""mwLA"">Russian remains perhaps the most important Eurasian travel language because English is very rarely spoken throughout the Russophone countries.</p>

<p id=""mwMw"">Consonants and vowels in Russian (and Slavic generally) are <i id=""mwNA"">soft</i> (palatalized) or <i id=""mwNQ"">hard</i>. Consonants are pronounced soft if followed by a soft vowel or the soft sign, else hard. Some consonants are always soft or always hard, regardless of the following vowel.</p>

<p id=""mwNg"">One important note: the cursive Russian alphabet looks very different from  the printed alphabet. The printed alphabet is rarely used when writing by hand. (The same goes with other Cyrillic-written languages.) On the upside, though, as a traveler, you are quite unlikely to have to read much handwritten Russian!</p>

<ul id=""mwYw""><li id=""mwZA"">The letters е, ю, and я sound like their counterparts э, у, and а</li>
<li id=""mwZQ"">The letter о sounds like the letter а when in the syllable before the stressed syllable, or like a schwa otherwise (eg. the e in English chick<b id=""mwZg"">e</b>n)</li>

<p id=""mwcA"">However, when traveling, you generally don't need to worry about reduction. Pronouncing all vowels as if they were stressed will seem like over-enunciation to a native speaker, but you'll be perfectly understood. </p>

<p id=""mwcQ"">Unfortunately, <b id=""mwcg"">ё</b> is very often written as <b id=""mwcw"">е</b>, which can cause problems for non-native speakers, since pronouncing one over the other can change the meaning of a word. Fortunately, books oriented toward beginners (like dictionaries, grammar books, literature for foreigners, etc.) always include the dots.</p>

<p id=""mwzg"">The letter sequence сч sounds like щ, and <i id=""mwzw"">not</i> like English que<b id=""mw0A"">sti</b>on.</p>

<p id=""mw0Q"">Be careful of letters such as В, Н, or Р which resemble a Latin letter but represent a different sound; they can be especially confusing for beginners.</p>

<p id=""mw0g"">When consonants are soft (they are either always soft, followed by a soft vowel, or have a soft sign), they become palatalized <span about=""#mwt41"" typeof=""mw:Transclusion"" id=""mw0w"" data-mw='{""parts"":[{""template"":{""target"":{""wt"":""IPA"",""href"":""./Template:IPA""},""params"":{""1"":{""wt"":""ʲ""}},""i"":0}}]}'>(</span><small about=""#mwt41""><a rel=""mw:WikiLink/Interwiki"" href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International%20Phonetic%20Alphabet"" title=""wikipedia:International Phonetic Alphabet"" class=""extiw"">IPA</a>:</small><span about=""#mwt41""> </span><span title=""This is an IPA transcription of pronunciation. See the soft sign below for more details.</p>

<p id=""mw1Q"">In the Middle Ages, these two characters (known as the <i id=""mw1g"">yer</i>) used to represent reduced vowels (pronounced like the unstressed vowels above), but now they merely indicate whether the preceding consonant is palatalized (""soft"") or not (""hard"").</p>

<dl id=""mw1w""><dt id=""mw2A"">ъ ’’</dt><dd id=""mw2Q"">the hard sign (very rarely used since 1918) - used to indicate that the preceding consonant is not palatalized (in a position where it otherwise would be). It is mostly used after prefixes <i id=""mw2g"">v-</i> (""in"") and <i id=""mw2w"">s-</i> (""out""). For example:</dd></dl>
<ul id=""mw3A""><li id=""mw3Q"">съёмка (s-yom-kah) - making a film</li>
<li id=""mw3g"">Сёмка (syom-kah) - familiar form for Simon</li>
<li id=""mw3w"">въезд (v-yezd) - vehicular entrance (not vyezd).</li></ul>

<p id=""mw4w"">This means that the consonant is pronounced while sticking the tip of your tongue behind your lower front teeth while raising the middle of your tongue to your palate. You don't really need to worry about this - while there are pairs of words that differ only by palatalization (some examples follow), it can be very difficult for beginners and probably not worth the effort. If you are planning on being in a situation where you will be speaking Russian for a long period of time, it may be wise to practice this. As mentioned, palatalization also occurs before soft vowels.</p>

</section><section data-mw-section-id=""5"" id=""mw7Q"" aria-labelledby=""Stress""><div class=""mw-heading mw-heading3""><h3 id=""Stress"">Stress</h3><span class=""mw-editsection""><span class=""mw-editsection-bracket"">[</span><a href=""/w/index.php?title=Russian_phrasebook&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5"" title=""Edit section: Stress""><span>edit</span></a><span class=""mw-editsection-bracket"">]</span></span></div>

<p id=""mw7g"">Although Russian is pronounced as it is spelled, stress is very unpredictable and stressing the wrong syllable can lead to misinterpretation; for that reason, almost every textbook and dictionary concerning the Russian language will put an accent mark (´) on the stressed syllable. It is, however, usually omitted in normal writing, so you will need to memorise the stressed syllable for each individual Russian word.</p>

</section></section><section data-mw-section-id=""6"" id=""mw7w"" aria-labelledby=""Grammar""><div class=""mw-heading mw-heading2""><h2 id=""Grammar"">Grammar</h2><span class=""mw-editsection""><span class=""mw-editsection-bracket"">[</span><a href=""/w/index.php?title=Russian_phrasebook&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6"" title=""Edit section: Grammar""><span>edit</span></a><span class=""mw-editsection-bracket"">]</span></span></div>

<p id=""mw8A"">Unless you intend to seriously study the language, learning Russian grammar on your trip is not realistic. But it can help to at least recognize that the following verb conjugations and noun/adjective declensions are used.</p>

<ul id=""mw8Q""><li id=""mw8g"">Russian nouns belong to one of three genders: masculine, feminine and neuter. However, like most other European languages but unlike English, inanimate objects are often assigned a gender other than neuter.</li></ul>

<ul id=""mw8w""><li id=""mw9A"">The second person pronoun <b id=""mw9Q"">вы</b> is the plural of <b id=""mw9g"">ты</b> and is also used, as in French, for <b id=""mw9w"">polite address</b> to one person.</li></ul>

Imperfective verbs indicate ongoing or uncompleted action; Perfective verbs indicated one-off or completed action. Conjugated perfective verbs are also used to indicated the future tense (future imperfective requires conjugation of the verb to be (быть) + imperfective infinitive). As a general rule (but only a very general rule), perfective verb forms are created by adding a prefix to the relevant imperfective verb.</dd></dl>

<div style=""font-weight:bold;"">How many names!</div>

<p>Russians take three names, a first name (имя), patronymic (отчество), and last name (фамилия). The unfamiliar patronymic is the name of one's father plus an ending of -ovich, -yevich, or -yich for men, -yevna, -ovna or -ichna for women. For example, if the father's name is Пётр (Peter), the patronymic would be Петрович (Petrovich) for a man, and Петровна (Petrovna) for a woman. To use someone's name informally, you can refer to them using the first or (less commonly) last name, while to do so formally would require either first name + patronymic, or a title + last name. For example, you can refer to President Vladimir Vladimirovich Putin (Президент Владимир Владимирович Путин) informally as Vladimir (if you know him personally) or just Putin. To refer to him more formally—and you generally should use the formal name in Russian—you would need to refer to him as Vladimir Vladimirovich or President Putin.</p>

<p>Last names also vary based on gender, often with an ""a"" added to the end of the male last name for the female version. For instance, the wife of former Russian prime minister Dmitry Medvedev is known as Svetlana Medvedev<b>a</b>.</p>

<p>But it gets even harder! Russians love diminutives, which are essentially nicknames or ""shortenings,"" for just about everything, including names. Most names can be shortened into three or four variants—""cute"" nicknames usually have an insert like -en'k, -echk, -ochk, -ushk, or -yush, like Sara → Sarochka (Сарочка) or Katya → Katyusha (Катюша). Obviously, you should avoid addressing people with these until you know them well. Here are some of the most common ones that might give you trouble:<br/><br/></p>

<li>Aleksei (Алексей) → Alyosha (Алёша), Lyosha (Лёша), Lyokha (Лёха)</li>
<li>Anatolii (Анатолий) → Tolya (Толя)</li>
<li>Vasilii (Василий) → Vasya (Вася)</li>
<li>Vladimir (Владимир) → Volodya (Володя), Vova (Вова)</li>

<div style=""font-weight:bold;"">Russian but not Russian</div>

<p>Russia is comprised of over 100 different ethnic groups, and while ethnic Russians form a majority, many Russian citizens are not ethnically Russian. Conversely, the other countries of the former <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Soviet_Union"" title=""Soviet Union"">Soviet Union</a> are home to ethnic Russian minorities who are not Russian citizens. In Russian, the concepts of Russian citizenship and ethnicity are represented by separate words. Русские (<i>ROOS-skee-yeh</i>) refers to someone who is ethnically Russian regardless of country of citizenship, while Россияне (<i>ruh-see-YAH-neh</i>) refers to someone who is a Russian citizen regardless of ethnicity.</p></td></tr>

</section><section data-mw-section-id=""16"" id=""mwA4g"" aria-labelledby=""Writing_time_and_date""><div class=""mw-heading mw-heading4""><h4 id=""Writing_time_and_date"">Writing time and date</h4><span class=""mw-editsection""><span class=""mw-editsection-bracket"">[</span><a href=""/w/index.php?title=Russian_phrasebook&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16"" title=""Edit section: Writing time and date""><span>edit</span></a><span class=""mw-editsection-bracket"">]</span></span></div>

<p id=""mwA4k"">Dates are written as <b id=""mwA4o"">day.month.year</b> (where <i id=""mwA4s"">day</i>, <i id=""mwA4w"">month</i> and <i id=""mwA40"">year</i> are numbers) or as <b id=""mwA44"">day month year</b> (where <i id=""mwA48"">day</i> and <i id=""mwA5A"">year</i> are numbers and <i id=""mwA5E"">month</i> is written in the genitive). If a person who was told this phrase is going to get off a bus, he says Да, выхожу (<i id=""mwA9g"">dah vyh-hah-ZHOO</i>) — Yes, I'm getting off. And you just go out after him. <div style=""font-weight:bold;"">Name changing</div>

<p>A lot of city, street, and other names changed following the fall of the Soviet Union. Sometimes for nationalist reasons in the former Soviet Republics, sometimes because the Soviet names seemed too weird and ideological, sometimes because names like ""Stalin"" no longer had such a good ring to them, and often because people wanted the old historic names back. This can present problems for travelers, especially when the street names have changed, and locals still sometimes refer to them by the old names. There isn't much to be done about this, but be sure to at least get your major city names straight:</p>
<ul><li>Leningrad → <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Saint_Petersburg"" title=""Saint Petersburg"">Saint Petersburg</a> (but the oblast is still Leningrad Oblast)</li>
