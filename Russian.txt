What should you know about Russian?|"<p id=""mwDw""><b id=""mwEA"">Russian</b> (русский) is a Slavic language spoken by 300+ million people world-wide. Most people living in <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Russia"" title=""Russia"" id=""mwEQ"">Russia</a> use it as a first language, and many people in <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Central_Asia"" title=""Central Asia"" id=""mwEg"">Central Asia</a>, the <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Caucasus"" title=""Caucasus"" id=""mwEw"">Caucasus</a>, and <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Europe"" title=""Europe"" id=""mwFA"">Eastern Europe</a> know it as a second language. It holds official status in Russia, <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Belarus"" title=""Belarus"" id=""mwFQ"">Belarus</a>, <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Kazakhstan"" title=""Kazakhstan"" id=""mwFg"">Kazakhstan</a>, <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Kyrgyzstan"" title=""Kyrgyzstan"" id=""mwFw"">Kyrgyzstan</a>, and the unrecognized <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Abkhazia"" title=""Abkhazia"" id=""mwGA"">Abkhazia</a>, <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Transnistria"" title=""Transnistria"" id=""mwGQ"">Transnistria</a> and <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/South_Ossetia"" title=""South Ossetia"" id=""mwGg"">South Ossetia</a>.</p>"
What should you know about Russian Russian in countries lacking official designation?|"<p id=""mwGw"">In countries lacking official designation for Russian, such as <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Ukraine"" title=""Ukraine"" id=""mwHA"">Ukraine</a>, <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Georgia_(country)"" title=""Georgia (country)"" id=""mwHQ"">Georgia</a> and the <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Baltic_States"" title=""Baltic States"" class=""mw-redirect"" id=""mwHg"">Baltic States</a> (where schooling in Russian was mandatory under the Soviet era), a solid majority of residents may speak it as a second language, and there are significant native-speaker minorities. However, due to its association with Soviet oppression, many residents in these countries may find it offensive to be addressed in Russian, and younger people will often prefer to speak English than Russian when communicating with foreigners.</p>
<p id=""mwHw"">Likewise, a similar dynamic exists in many of the formerly communist countries of Europe such as <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Poland"" title=""Poland"" id=""mwIA"">Poland</a>, the <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Czech_Republic"" title=""Czech Republic"" id=""mwIQ"">Czech Republic</a> and <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Hungary"" title=""Hungary"" id=""mwIg"">Hungary</a>, in which Russian was a compulsory second language in schools under communist rule.</p>
<p id=""mwIw"">Russian remains the <i id=""mwJA"">lingua franca</i> of choice throughout the former Soviet <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Central_Asia"" title=""Central Asia"" id=""mwJQ"">Central Asian</a> states, <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Armenia"" title=""Armenia"" id=""mwJg"">Armenia</a>, <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Azerbaijan"" title=""Azerbaijan"" id=""mwJw"">Azerbaijan</a> and <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Moldova"" title=""Moldova"" id=""mwKA"">Moldova</a>, where it is effectively the language of commerce, government, and travel (despite lacking official status).</p>
<p id=""mwKQ"">It is, to a lesser extent, an important language in <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Mongolia"" title=""Mongolia"" id=""mwKg"">Mongolia</a>, where it is a compulsory second language in schools, and is the most widely spoken foreign language. Russian has also become the third most widely spoken language in <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Israel"" title=""Israel"" id=""mwKw"">Israel</a>, owing to a massive exodus of Jews from Russia and other ex-Soviet countries in the late 20th and early 21st centuries.</p>
<p id=""mwLA"">Russian remains perhaps the most important Eurasian travel language because English is very rarely spoken throughout the Russophone countries.</p>

What should you know about Consonants and vowels in Russian?|"<p id=""mwMw"">Consonants and vowels in Russian (and Slavic generally) are <i id=""mwNA"">soft</i> (palatalized) or <i id=""mwNQ"">hard</i>. Consonants are pronounced soft if followed by a soft vowel or the soft sign, else hard. Some consonants are always soft or always hard, regardless of the following vowel.</p>"
What is important to note about the Russian alphabet when writing by hand?|"<p id=""mwNg"">One important note: the cursive Russian alphabet looks very different from  the printed alphabet. The printed alphabet is rarely used when writing by hand. (The same goes with other Cyrillic-written languages.) On the upside, though, as a traveler, you are quite unlikely to have to read much handwritten Russian!</p>

What should you know about vowel reduction in Russian?|"<p id=""mwYg"">Russian, like English, has something called vowel reduction, where the vowels take on different sounds if they are not in the stressed syllable of the word. <li id=""mwZQ"">The letter о sounds like the letter а when in the syllable before the stressed syllable, or like a schwa otherwise (eg. the e in English chick<b id=""mwZg"">e</b>n)</li>

What should you know about names in Russian?|"<p>Russians take three names, a first name (имя), patronymic (отчество), and last name (фамилия). The unfamiliar patronymic is the name of one's father plus an ending of -ovich, -yevich, or -yich for men, -yevna, -ovna or -ichna for women. For example, if the father's name is Пётр (Peter), the patronymic would be Петрович (Petrovich) for a man, and Петровна (Petrovna) for a woman. To use someone's name informally, you can refer to them using the first or (less commonly) last name, while to do so formally would require either first name + patronymic, or a title + last name. For example, you can refer to President Vladimir Vladimirovich Putin (Президент Владимир Владимирович Путин) informally as Vladimir (if you know him personally) or just Putin. To refer to him more formally—and you generally should use the formal name in Russian—you would need to refer to him as Vladimir Vladimirovich or President Putin.</p>

<p>Last names also vary based on gender, often with an ""a"" added to the end of the male last name for the female version. For instance, the wife of former Russian prime minister Dmitry Medvedev is known as Svetlana Medvedev<b>a</b>.</p>

<p>But it gets even harder! Russians love diminutives, which are essentially nicknames or ""shortenings,"" for just about everything, including names. Obviously, you should avoid addressing people with these until you know them well. Here are some of the most common ones that might give you trouble:<br/><br/></p>
