<p id=""mwDQ""><b id=""mwDg"">French</b> (<i id=""mwDw"">français</i>) is a Romance language (descended from Latin), and one of the most widely spoken languages in the world: 277 million people speak French, including about 100 million native speakers. While the French language originated in <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/France"" title=""France"" id=""mwEA"">France</a>, in modern times it is spoken on every continent; it is an official language of 29 countries, an important business, cultural, or minority language in dozens of other countries and regions, and is used officially by scores of international organisations including the United Nations, the European Union, and the International Olympic Committee. French was the main international lingua franca well into the 20th century, and at one point, French was the language spoken in most of the royal courts of Europe. To this day, it remains <i id=""mwEQ"">de rigueur</i> for educated people in many societies around the world to have some level of basic French ability.</p>

<p id=""mwEg"">French is the sole official language of France, including all of its overseas departments and territories, and is the only language you will need to communicate with French nationals. Beyond France, French is widely spoken in many nearby countries in <b id=""mwEw"">Europe</b>, including the southern half of <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Belgium"" title=""Belgium"" id=""mwFA"">Belgium</a> (<a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Wallonia"" title=""Wallonia"" id=""mwFQ"">Wallonia</a> and <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Brussels"" title=""Brussels"" id=""mwFg"">Brussels</a>), western <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Switzerland"" title=""Switzerland"" id=""mwFw"">Switzerland</a>, <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Monaco"" title=""Monaco"" id=""mwGA"">Monaco</a>, <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Luxembourg"" title=""Luxembourg"" id=""mwGQ"">Luxembourg</a>, and the <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Aosta_Valley"" title=""Aosta Valley"" id=""mwGg"">Aosta Valley</a> of northwestern <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Italy"" title=""Italy"" id=""mwGw"">Italy</a>. A small number of second-language speakers are also found on the <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Channel_Islands"" title=""Channel Islands"" id=""mwHA"">Channel Island</a> of <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Jersey"" title=""Jersey"" id=""mwHQ"">Jersey</a> (where a dialect of Norman extremely similar to French persists) and in the tiny Pyrenean country of <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Andorra"" title=""Andorra"" id=""mwHg"">Andorra</a>. </p>

<p id=""mwHw"">In the <b id=""mwIA"">Americas</b>, French is spoken primarily in the Canadian provinces of <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Quebec"" title=""Quebec"" id=""mwIQ"">Quebec</a>, <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/New_Brunswick"" title=""New Brunswick"" id=""mwIg"">New Brunswick</a>, the northern and eastern parts of <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Ontario"" title=""Ontario"" id=""mwIw"">Ontario</a>, around the Winnipeg area of <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Manitoba"" title=""Manitoba"" id=""mwJA"">Manitoba</a>, and the city of Whitehorse in <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Yukon"" title=""Yukon"" id=""mwJQ"">Yukon</a>. Indeed, <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Canada"" title=""Canada"" id=""mwJg"">Canada</a> is an officially bilingual nation and there are Francophone enclaves in almost every province, though outside of the four provinces and territory mentioned, it's quite rare to encounter anyone in Canada who speaks more than a few words of French without hunting down off-the-beaten-track French-speaking communities. French is also spoken in a few parts of the <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/United_States"" title=""United States"" id=""mwJw"">United States</a>, namely parts of <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Louisiana"" title=""Louisiana"" id=""mwKA"">Louisiana</a> and northern <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Maine"" title=""Maine"" id=""mwKQ"">Maine</a>, <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/New_Hampshire"" title=""New Hampshire"" id=""mwKg"">New Hampshire</a>, and <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Vermont"" title=""Vermont"" id=""mwKw"">Vermont</a>. In the Caribbean, French is an official language of <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Haiti"" title=""Haiti"" id=""mwLA"">Haiti</a>, a former colonial possession of France. The Americas also host the French departments of <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Martinique"" title=""Martinique"" id=""mwLQ"">Martinique</a>, <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Guadeloupe"" title=""Guadeloupe"" id=""mwLg"">Guadeloupe</a>, and <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/French_Guiana"" title=""French Guiana"" id=""mwLw"">French Guiana</a>, plus the overseas collectivities of <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Saint_Pierre_and_Miquelon"" title=""Saint Pierre and Miquelon"" id=""mwMA"">Saint Pierre and Miquelon</a>, <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Saint_Barthélemy"" title=""Saint Barthélemy"" class=""mw-redirect"" id=""mwMQ"">Saint Barthélemy</a>, and the northern half of <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Saint_Martin"" title=""Saint Martin"" id=""mwMg"">Saint Martin</a>.</p>

<p id=""mwMw""><b id=""mwNA"">Elsewhere</b>, French is an official language of many former French and Belgian colonies in Africa, like <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Cameroon"" title=""Cameroon"" id=""mwNQ"">Cameroon</a>, the <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Democratic_Republic_of_the_Congo"" title=""Democratic Republic of the Congo"" id=""mwNg"">Democratic Republic of the Congo</a>, and the <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Republic_of_the_Congo"" title=""Republic of the Congo"" id=""mwNw"">Republic of the Congo</a>, and is unofficial but carries prestige in others, namely <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Algeria"" title=""Algeria"" id=""mwOA"">Algeria</a>, <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Tunisia"" title=""Tunisia"" id=""mwOQ"">Tunisia</a>, and <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Morocco"" title=""Morocco"" id=""mwOg"">Morocco</a>. It is an important administrative, educational, and cultural language in the former French Southeast Asian possessions of <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Vietnam"" title=""Vietnam"" id=""mwOw"">Vietnam</a>, <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Laos"" title=""Laos"" id=""mwPA"">Laos</a>, and <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Cambodia"" title=""Cambodia"" id=""mwPQ"">Cambodia</a>. In the Indian Ocean, <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Réunion"" title=""Réunion"" id=""mwPg"">Réunion</a> and <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Mayotte"" title=""Mayotte"" id=""mwPw"">Mayotte</a> are French overseas departments, while French is also an official language in <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Mauritius"" title=""Mauritius"" id=""mwQA"">Mauritius</a>, the <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Seychelles"" title=""Seychelles"" id=""mwQQ"">Seychelles</a>, and <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Comoros"" title=""Comoros"" id=""mwQg"">Comoros</a>. In Oceania, <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/New_Caledonia"" title=""New Caledonia"" id=""mwQw"">New Caledonia</a>, <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/French_Polynesia"" title=""French Polynesia"" id=""mwRA"">French Polynesia</a>, and <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Wallis_and_Futuna"" title=""Wallis and Futuna"" id=""mwRQ"">Wallis and Futuna</a> remain overseas territories of France, and French is also one of the official languages of <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Vanuatu"" title=""Vanuatu"" id=""mwRg"">Vanuatu</a>.</p>

<p id=""mwRw"">The French Wikivoyage's page <a rel=""mw:WikiLink/Interwiki"" href=""https://fr.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Francophonie"" title=""fr:Francophonie"" class=""extiw"" id=""mwSA"">Francophonie</a> can help you locate French-speaking regions.</p>

<p id=""mwTw"">French spelling is <b id=""mwUA"">not very phonetic</b>. The same letter used in two different words can make two different sounds, and many letters are not pronounced at all. The good news, though, is that French generally has more regular pronunciation rules than English. This means that with sufficient practice, one can generally pronounce written French fairly accurately. However, the large number of homophones and silent letters make it such that attempting to write down spoken French often results in spelling mistakes, even for native speakers. Some rules are as follows:</p>

<ul id=""mwUQ""><li id=""mwUg""><b id=""mwUw"">Stress</b> is fairly even in French, but the stress almost always falls on the last syllable. In words where the stress falls on an earlier syllable, it's not uncommon to hear the last syllable or sound of some words cut off or ""swallowed."" For example, <i id=""mwVA"">Possible</i> can sound like <i id=""mwVQ"">poss-EEB</i> and <i id=""mwVg"">hymne</i> can sound like <i id=""mwVw"">eem</i>. This is particularly noticeable in Quebec, but exists in other accents too.</li>
<li id=""mwWA"">The <b id=""mwWQ"">final consonant</b> of a word is usually silent except for c, f, l, q, and r. Thus, <i id=""mwWg"">allez</i> (go) is pronounced <i id=""mwWw"">al-AY</i>, not <i id=""mwXA"">al-AYZ</i>; <i id=""mwXQ"">tard</i> (late) is pronounced <i id=""mwXg"">tar</i>, not <i id=""mwXw"">tard</i>.</li>
<li id=""mwYA"">If the next word begins with a vowel, a consonant may be pronounced; this is called <i id=""mwYQ""><b id=""mwYg"">liaison</b></i>. For example, <i id=""mwYw"">allez-y</i> (≈ go ahead / off you go), is pronounced <i id=""mwZA"">al-ay-ZEE</i>, while <i id=""mwZQ"">mes amis</i> (my friends) is pronounced <i id=""mwZg"">MEZ-ah-MEE</i> .</li>
<li id=""mwZw"">A <b id=""mwaA"">final e</b> is also usually silent if the word has more than one syllable, except in parts of southern France and when singing or reciting poetry.</li></ul>

<p id=""mwaQ"">Guess what? Those pronunciation ""rules"" you just read have a ton of <b id=""mwag"">exceptions</b>! For example, the rule that a final r is pronounced is not true in the combination ""-er"", normally found in verb infinitives; this letter combination is pronounced <i id=""mwaw"">ay</i>. The plural ending ""-ent"" for verbs is silent (except for the t, in cases of liaison), even though it is pronounced when it appears in other words. Sometimes, whether to pronounce the final consonant of a word is denoted by its grammatical function; for example, the final ""s"" in <i id=""mwbA"">tous</i> (all) is silent when used as an adjective, but pronounced when used as a pronoun, while the final ""f"" in nouns such as <i id=""mwbQ"">cerf</i> (deer) and <i id=""mwbg"">œuf</i> (egg) is pronounced in the singular form, but in the plural form (<i id=""mwbw"">cerfs</i>, <i id=""mwcA"">œufs</i>) the ""f"" and the ""s"" are both silent.</p>

<p id=""mwcQ"">A final warning: for many French words, it is impossible to write something which an English speaker can easily read and reproduce without compromising on the French pronunciation. This means that <b id=""mwcg"">the pronunciation guides in this very phrasebook are open to interpretation!</b> Use the transliteration provided with each phrase as a guide to <i id=""mwcw"">liaison</i>, but refer to the following letter lists in order to correctly pronounce the vowels and consonants.</p>

<section data-mw-section-id=""2"" id=""mwdA"" aria-labelledby=""Vowels""><div class=""mw-heading mw-heading3""><h3 id=""Vowels"">Vowels</h3><span class=""mw-editsection""><span class=""mw-editsection-bracket"">[</span><a href=""/w/index.php?title=French_phrasebook&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2"" title=""Edit section: Vowels""><span>edit</span></a><span class=""mw-editsection-bracket"">]</span></span></div>

<p id=""mwdQ"">Vowels in French can have <b id=""mwdg"">accent marks</b>, which generally have no noticeable impact on pronunciation, but they often distinguish between homophones in writing (<i id=""mwdw"">ou</i>, meaning ""or"", and <i id=""mweA"">où</i>, meaning ""where"", are pronounced the same). The only really important ones which affect pronunciation are é, è, and ê, which are called <i id=""mweQ"">e accent aigu</i>, <i id=""mweg"">e accent grave</i>, and <i id=""mwew"">e accent circonflexe</i>, respectively. </section><section data-mw-section-id=""7"" id=""mwAWE"" aria-labelledby=""Exceptions""><div class=""mw-heading mw-heading3""><h3 id=""Exceptions"">Exceptions</h3><span class=""mw-editsection""><span class=""mw-editsection-bracket"">[</span><a href=""/w/index.php?title=French_phrasebook&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7"" title=""Edit section: Exceptions""><span>edit</span></a><span class=""mw-editsection-bracket"">]</span></span></div>
<ul id=""mwAWI""><li id=""mwAWM"">When there is an accent mark on ""e"", it prevents diphthongs. Letters should be pronounced separately, following the rule for the accented letter. Example: <i id=""mwAWQ"">réunion</i> (meeting).</li>

When the e is marked with an acute accent (as in <i id=""mwAWw"">géologie</i>) it is pronounced the normal way.</li></ul>

<section data-mw-section-id=""9"" id=""mwAXM"" aria-labelledby=""Gender_and_its_complications""><div class=""mw-heading mw-heading3""><h3 id=""Gender_and_its_complications"">Gender and its complications</h3><span class=""mw-editsection""><span class=""mw-editsection-bracket"">[</span><a href=""/w/index.php?title=French_phrasebook&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9"" title=""Edit section: Gender and its complications""><span>edit</span></a><span class=""mw-editsection-bracket"">]</span></span></div>
<p id=""mwAXQ"">French nouns are divided into two different <b id=""mwAXU"">genders</b>: masculine and feminine. Unlike in English, all inanimate objects have a gender assigned to them: for example, <i id=""mwAXY"">pain</i> (bread) is masculine, while <i id=""mwAXc"">confiture</i> (jam) is feminine. The grammatical gender of nouns denoting persons generally follows the person's natural sex; for instance, <i id=""mwAXg"">mère</i> (mother) is feminine, while <i id=""mwAXk"">père</i> (father) is masculine. However, some nouns are always of the same gender regardless of the natural gender of the person they are referring to: <i id=""mwAXo"">personne</i> is always feminine even if the person in question is a man. </p>

<p id=""mwAXs"">It is not always easy to tell at a glance which gender a noun is but, in general, if it ends in a consonant, or by the letter combinations <i id=""mwAXw"">-age</i>, <i id=""mwAX0"">-au</i>, <i id=""mwAX4"">-é</i>, <i id=""mwAX8"">-ège</i>, <i id=""mwAYA"">ème</i>, or <i id=""mwAYE"">-isme</i> / <i id=""mwAYI"">-iste</i>, or is a foreign (particularly English) loanword, it's likely to be masculine. On the other hand, if a noun ends in <i id=""mwAYM"">-ace</i>, <i id=""mwAYQ"">-ance</i> / <i id=""mwAYU"">-ence</i>, <i id=""mwAYY"">-ée</i>, <i id=""mwAYc"">-elle</i> / <i id=""mwAYg"">-erre</i> / <i id=""mwAYk"">-esse</i> / <i id=""mwAYo"">-ette</i>, <i id=""mwAYs"">-ie</i>, <i id=""mwAYw"">-ice</i>, <i id=""mwAY0"">-ine</i>, <i id=""mwAY4"">-ise</i>, <i id=""mwAY8"">-que</i>, or <i id=""mwAZA"">-tion / sion</i>, it's probably feminine. There are plenty of exceptions, however.</p>

<p id=""mwAZE"">The singular <b id=""mwAZI"">definite article</b> (""the"" in English) of each noun depends on its gender: <i id=""mwAZM"">le</i> (m), <i id=""mwAZQ"">la</i> (f) or <i id=""mwAZU"">l’</i> (before all singular nouns starting with a vowel and some starting with ""h"", regardless of gender). The plural definite article for both genders is <i id=""mwAZY"">les</i>. Thus: </p>
<ul id=""mwAZc""><li id=""mwAZg"">le garçon – the boy → les garçons – the boys</li>
<li id=""mwAZk"">la fille – the girl → les filles – the girls</li>
<li id=""mwAZo"">l'homme – the man → les hommes – the men</li></ul>

<p id=""mwAZs"">The singular <b id=""mwAZw"">indefinite article</b> (""a"" and ""an"" in English) also corresponds to the noun's gender: <i id=""mwAZ0"">un</i> for masculine and <i id=""mwAZ4"">une</i> for feminine. Unlike English, French has a plural indefinite article – <i id=""mwAZ8"">des</i>, which works for both genders – and three <b id=""mwAaA"">partitive articles</b> – <i id=""mwAaE"">du</i> (m), <i id=""mwAaI"">de la</i> (f), and <i id=""mwAaM"">de l’</i> (before vowels and some instances of the letter ""h""), which precede uncountable nouns (all of these can mean ""of the"", but they are sometimes needed where no article would be used in English). Thus:</p>
<ul id=""mwAaQ""><li id=""mwAaU"">un homme – a man → des hommes – men</li>
<li id=""mwAaY"">une femme – a woman → des femmes – women</li>
<li id=""mwAac"">du vin – wine</li>
<li id=""mwAag"">de la confiture – jam</li>
<li id=""mwAak"">de l'eau – water</li></ul>

<p id=""mwAao"">Similarly, the <b id=""mwAas"">third person pronouns</b> also depend on the grammatical gender of the subject: <i id=""mwAaw"">il</i> (m – he or it) or <i id=""mwAa0"">elle</i> (f – she or it), with <i id=""mwAa4"">ils</i> and <i id=""mwAa8"">elles</i> respectively being the masculine and feminine plurals (they). When there are groups of mixed-gender people or objects, <i id=""mwAbA"">ils</i> is always used.</p>

</section><section data-mw-section-id=""10"" id=""mwAbE"" aria-labelledby=""Formal_and_informal_speech""><div class=""mw-heading mw-heading3""><h3 id=""Formal_and_informal_speech"">Formal and informal speech</h3><span class=""mw-editsection""><span class=""mw-editsection-bracket"">[</span><a href=""/w/index.php?title=French_phrasebook&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10"" title=""Edit section: Formal and informal speech""><span>edit</span></a><span class=""mw-editsection-bracket"">]</span></span></div>
<p id=""mwAbI"">In French, there are two equivalents of the English word <b id=""mwAbM"">""you""</b>. When addressing one person you know well such as a family member or a friend, plus any time you speak to one child or one animal, the word to use will be <i id=""mwAbQ"">tu</i>. In <i id=""mwAbU""><b id=""mwAbY"">all</b></i> other situations, including when addressing a group of people regardless of who they are, the word to use will be <i id=""mwAbc"">vous</i>. This means that in practice, as a traveller and novice French speaker, most of the time you will be using <i id=""mwAbg"">vous</i>. It is important to know the distinction, as while addressing a pet dog with the <i id=""mwAbk"">vous</i> form might just raise a chuckle, using <i id=""mwAbo"">tu</i> with somebody you've just met is inappropriate and may offend the person whom you are addressing. After initially using the <i id=""mwAbs"">vous</i> form, a person may say to you <i id=""mwAbw"">""On peut se tutoyer""</i>; this is a polite invitation for you to use the <i id=""mwAb0"">tu</i> form with them.</p>

<p id=""mwAb4"">The default <b id=""mwAb8"">title</b> used when addressing a man is <i id=""mwAcA"">monsieur</i>, while a woman would be addressed as <i id=""mwAcE"">madame</i>. <i id=""mwAcI"">Mademoiselle</i> was traditionally used to address young, unmarried women, but this is now controversial and arguably sexist, so unless the other person tells you otherwise, it is best to default to <i id=""mwAcM"">madame</i>. The respective plurals are <i id=""mwAcQ"">messieurs</i> and <i id=""mwAcU"">mesdames</i>, so the French equivalent of ""ladies and gentlemen"" is ""<i id=""mwAcY"">mesdames et messieurs</i>"", though often in speech this is rendered as ""<i id=""mwAcc"">messieurs-dames</i>"".</p>

</section><section data-mw-section-id=""11"" id=""mwAcg"" aria-labelledby=""Verbs""><div class=""mw-heading mw-heading3""><h3 id=""Verbs"">Verbs</h3><span class=""mw-editsection""><span class=""mw-editsection-bracket"">[</span><a href=""/w/index.php?title=French_phrasebook&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11"" title=""Edit section: Verbs""><span>edit</span></a><span class=""mw-editsection-bracket"">]</span></span></div>

<p id=""mwAck"">In a manner similar to many other Romance languages, French <b id=""mwAco"">verbs</b> all end in either <i id=""mwAcs"">-er</i>, <i id=""mwAcw"">-ir</i>, or <i id=""mwAc0"">-re</i> in their infinitive forms, for example <i id=""mwAc4"">écouter</i> (to listen), <i id=""mwAc8"">finir</i> (to finish), and <i id=""mwAdA"">vendre</i> (to sell). Verbs in French <b id=""mwAdE"">conjugate</b> differently according to tense, mood, aspect and voice. This means that there are many more possible conjugations for French verbs than English verbs, and learning how to conjugate each verb in different scenarios can be a challenge for English speakers. Fortunately for you, the vast majority of verbs follow a regular conjugation pattern. </p>

<p id=""mwAj8"">Common regular verbs that you can use the above models for include: <i id=""mwAkA"">aimer</i> (to like/love), <i id=""mwAkE"">chercher</i> (to search), <i id=""mwAkI"">parler</i> (to speak), <i id=""mwAkM"">demander</i> (to ask), <i id=""mwAkQ"">trouver</i> (to find), <i id=""mwAkU"">penser</i> (to think), <i id=""mwAkY"">visiter</i> (to visit), <i id=""mwAkc"">voyager</i> (to travel), <i id=""mwAkg"">choisir</i> (to choose), <i id=""mwAkk"">attendre</i> (to wait), <i id=""mwAko"">perdre</i> (to lose), and <i id=""mwAks"">répondre</i> (to answer).</p>

<p id=""mwAkw"">Some verbs are <b id=""mwAk0"">irregular</b>, meaning that they use different roots when conjugated. The good news is that irregular verbs are very much in the minority. </section></section><section data-mw-section-id=""12"" id=""mwAqY"" aria-labelledby=""International_varieties_of_French""><div class=""mw-heading mw-heading2""><h2 id=""International_varieties_of_French"">International varieties of French</h2><span class=""mw-editsection""><span class=""mw-editsection-bracket"">[</span><a href=""/w/index.php?title=French_phrasebook&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12"" title=""Edit section: International varieties of French""><span>edit</span></a><span class=""mw-editsection-bracket"">]</span></span></div>
<figure typeof=""mw:File/Thumb"" id=""mwAqc""><a href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/File:Guadeloupe_creole_2010-03-30.JPG"" class=""mw-file-description"" id=""mwAqg""><img resource=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/File:Guadeloupe_creole_2010-03-30.JPG"" src=""//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Guadeloupe_creole_2010-03-30.JPG/330px-Guadeloupe_creole_2010-03-30.JPG?utm_source=en.wikivoyage.org&amp;utm_campaign=parser&amp;utm_content=thumbnail"" decoding=""async"" data-file-width=""3872"" data-file-height=""2592"" data-file-type=""bitmap"" height=""201"" width=""300"" srcset=""//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Guadeloupe_creole_2010-03-30.JPG/960px-Guadeloupe_creole_2010-03-30.JPG?utm_source=en.wikivoyage.org&amp;utm_campaign=parser&amp;utm_content=thumbnail 2x"" class=""mw-file-element"" id=""mwAqk""/></a><figcaption id=""mwAqo"">""Levez le pied, il y a des enfants qui jouent ici<span typeof=""mw:DisplaySpace"" id=""mwAqs""> </span>!"" – Lift your foot [off the gas pedal], there are children playing here! (Guadeloupe Creole)</figcaption></figure>

<p id=""mwAqw"">For its size, <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/France"" title=""France"" id=""mwAq0"">France</a> is quite a linguistically-diverse country. Aside from languages which are very clearly separate from French (e.g. <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Basque"" title=""Basque"" class=""mw-redirect"" id=""mwAq4"">Basque</a> and <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Breton"" title=""Breton"" class=""mw-redirect"" id=""mwAq8"">Breton</a>), there is a whole slew of local <i id=""mwArA"">parlers</i> (e.g. Angevin, Lorrain, Norman, Picard, Savoyard...) which are just similar enough to standard French that, depending on whom you ask, they can be considered either separate languages in their own right, or simply dialects (<i id=""mwArE"">patois</i>) of the mother tongue. These local languages/dialects also influence the accents of standard French within their region, from the strange vowels and increased nasalisation of the far north to the 'singing' accents of the deep south.</p>

<p id=""mwArI"">The varieties of French which are spoken in Belgium and Switzerland differ slightly from the French spoken in France, though they are similar enough to be mutually intelligible. In particular, the numbering system in French-speaking <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Belgium"" title=""Belgium"" id=""mwArM"">Belgium</a> and <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Switzerland"" title=""Switzerland"" id=""mwArQ"">Switzerland</a> has some slight peculiarities that are different from the French spoken in France, and the pronunciation of some words is slightly different. Nevertheless, all French-speaking Belgians and Swiss would have learned standard French in school, so they would be able to understand you even if you used the standard French numbering system.</p>

<p id=""mwArU"">Aside from Europe and Canada (see below), many French-speaking regions have incorporated the words of local languages, and on occasion have formed distinctive dialects or languages known as <b id=""mwArY"">creoles</b>.  French-based creoles today enjoy wide use and often official status in the <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Seychelles"" title=""Seychelles"" id=""mwArc"">Seychelles</a>, <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Mauritius"" title=""Mauritius"" id=""mwArg"">Mauritius</a>, <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/New_Caledonia"" title=""New Caledonia"" id=""mwArk"">New Caledonia</a>, <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Haiti"" title=""Haiti"" id=""mwAro"">Haiti</a> (see <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Haitian_Creole_phrasebook"" title=""Haitian Creole phrasebook"" id=""mwArs"">Haitian creole</a>), <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Réunion"" title=""Réunion"" id=""mwArw"">Réunion</a>, and the French overseas territories in the Antilles. A dialect of French known as <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Louisiana"" title=""Louisiana"" id=""mwAr0"">Louisiana</a> French or Cajun, which is similar to the Acadian French spoken in parts of New Brunswick, and a distinct French-based creole known as Louisiana Creole are both still spoken by some residents in the southern U.S. state, while parts of <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/New_England"" title=""New England"" id=""mwAr4"">New England</a> near the Canadian border are home to speakers of a dialect known as New England French, which bears many similarities to Québécois. </p>

<section data-mw-section-id=""13"" id=""mwAr8"" aria-labelledby=""Canada""><div class=""mw-heading mw-heading3""><h3 id=""Canada"">Canada</h3><span class=""mw-editsection""><span class=""mw-editsection-bracket"">[</span><a href=""/w/index.php?title=French_phrasebook&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13"" title=""Edit section: Canada""><span>edit</span></a><span class=""mw-editsection-bracket"">]</span></span></div>

<p id=""mwAsY"">There are many phonological and lexical differences between the French spoken in Quebec and that spoken in France. Quebec has retained many 18th- &amp; 19th-century French words, while in France the language has moved on, as well as incorporating many English words in the modern era. On the other side, Québécois borrowed English terms from their Anglophone neighbours as early as the 19th century, but the onset of the ""Quiet Revolution"" and the Quebec sovereignty movement in the 1960s led to laws that strictly limited the usage and influence of English in the public sphere, with the result that, etymologically speaking, Quebec French is in many ways more ""purely"" French than that spoken in France. For instance, the <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Fast_food_in_the_United_States_and_Canada#Chicken"" title=""Fast food in the United States and Canada"" id=""mwAsc"">fast-food chain</a> called ""Kentucky Fried Chicken"" (KFC) in both the United States and France is known in Quebec as <i id=""mwAsg"">Poulet Frit Kentucky</i> (PFK). In addition, menus are all translated, resulting in <i id=""mwAsk"">le Joyeux Festin</i> at McDonald's in Quebec, rather than <i id=""mwAso"">le Happy Meal</i> as in the rest of the French-speaking world.</p>

<p id=""mwAzI"">Each of Canada's other provinces has a Francophone population, who are not Québécois. Some of these groups have been settled for hundreds of years. Another distinct dialect of French, known as Acadian French, is spoken commonly in parts of <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/New_Brunswick"" title=""New Brunswick"" id=""mwAzM"">New Brunswick</a>, with smaller populations in Nova Scotia and Prince Edward Island. Many of these people were expelled by the British during the French and Indian War, and settled in <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Louisiana"" title=""Louisiana"" id=""mwAzQ"">Louisiana</a>, where they would later become known as the Cajuns.</p>

<p id=""mwAzU"">Nevertheless, all Francophone Canadians, including Québécois, learn standard French in school, and most of the differences between the two varieties are limited to informal speech. This means that while you may not understand conversation among locals, they will be able to converse with you in standard French if required.</p>

</section></section><section data-mw-section-id=""14"" id=""mwAzY"" aria-labelledby=""Cognates_in_English""><div class=""mw-heading mw-heading2""><h2 id=""Cognates_in_English"">Cognates in English</h2><span class=""mw-editsection""><span class=""mw-editsection-bracket"">[</span><a href=""/w/index.php?title=French_phrasebook&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14"" title=""Edit section: Cognates in English""><span>edit</span></a><span class=""mw-editsection-bracket"">]</span></span></div>
<p id=""mwAzc"">English has borrowed a huge number of French words since the Norman conquest of England in 1066 and also shares a large number of cognates that English borrowed directly from Latin, so you will recognize many French words, especially after you get used to French pronunciation. For example, a large number of words ending in -tion are spelled the same way in French, although the pronunciation is different. Among these words are <i id=""mwAzg"">production</i>, <i id=""mwAzk"">introduction</i>, <i id=""mwAzo"">participation</i> and <i id=""mwAzs"">indication</i>. Also, words ending in -ty in English normally end in -té in French, including <i id=""mwAzw"">personnalité</i> (personality), <i id=""mwAz0"">université</i> (university), <i id=""mwAz4"">responsabilité</i> (responsibility) and <i id=""mwAz8"">sécurité</i> (safety/security). Another signpost to look for is words that end in -ous in English usually end in -eux (masculine singular) or -euse (feminine singular) in French. Examples include <i id=""mwA0A"">sérieux/sérieuse</i> (serious), <i id=""mwA0E"">dangereux/dangereuse</i> (dangerous) and <i id=""mwA0I"">joyeux/joyeuse</i> (happy/joyous).</p>

<p id=""mwA0M"">When it comes to culinary terms, the meat of specific animals is often referred to using a loan word from French, while the animal itself is referred to using the original Germanic term. For example English pork is cognate with French <i id=""mwA0Q"">porc</i>, English beef is cognate with French <i id=""mwA0U"">bœuf</i>, and English mutton is cognate with French <i id=""mwA0Y"">mouton</i>. In French, the distinction between animal and meat isn't made.</p>

<p id=""mwA0c"">There are many other French words that were borrowed with little or no change in spelling into English, such as <i id=""mwA0g"">hommage</i> (homage), <i id=""mwA0k"">instrument</i> (instrument), <i id=""mwA0o"">concert</i> (concert), <i id=""mwA0s"">préférence</i> (preference) and <i id=""mwA0w"">honneur</i> (honour). There are sometimes differences in meaning or usage between the languages, though, as the French word <i id=""mwA00"">cuisine</i> means not only a style of cooking as in English usage but also a kitchen; <i id=""mwA04"">café</i>, similarly, means ""coffee"" and not just a place where you can have some along with food; and the verb <i id=""mwA08"">conduire</i>, whose noun form is <i id=""mwA1A"">conduction</i>, is used more often to mean ""to drive"" than ""to conduct"". Similarly, <i id=""mwA1E"">un conducteur</i> would usually mean ""a driver"" or ""an operator"", rather than ""a conductor"". <i id=""mwA1I"">Chauffeur</i> is another generic term for a driver, not necessarily a private driver as in English. As well as a place to stay, <i id=""mwA1M"">hôtel</i> can refer to many types of large urban building in French, so <i id=""mwA1Q"">un hôtel particulier</i> is a grand townhouse or mansion, <i id=""mwA1U"">l'hôtel de ville</i> is the city or town hall, while <i id=""mwA1Y"">l'hôtel du parlement</i> is the parliament building. The <i id=""mwA1c"">entrée</i> isn't just a course in a meal, but also the word for any kind of entrance. By the way, <i id=""mwA1g"">entrée</i> refers to a smaller dish before the main course in French, and this original meaning is retained in Australian and British English, but has shifted to refer to the main course itself in American and Canadian English. <i id=""mwA1k"">Sensible</i> typically means ""sensitive"", and not ""sensible"" in the usual English sense (you can use a word like <i id=""mwA1o"">raisonnable</i>, the cognate for ""reasonable"", instead). Also, watch out for the French word <i id=""mwA1s"">préservatif</i>, which does not mean a preservative but a condom! If you want to refer to preservatives in food, use the word <i id=""mwA1w"">conservateurs</i>. Disclaimers aside, there are quite a lot of French words you can guess correctly by understanding the equivalents and seeing some patterns of how similar words differ between the languages.</p>

<p id=""mwA10"">There are also some English words that are commonly used in French, including <i id=""mwA14"">week-end</i> and <i id=""mwA18"">sandwich</i>, and with slightly less equivalency, <i id=""mwA2A"">parking</i> for carpark and <i id=""mwA2E"">shampooing</i> for shampoo. The most common word for ""tracksuit"" is <i id=""mwA2I"">jogging</i>, a word which can also refer to jogging for exercise; the latter is also called <i id=""mwA2M"">footing</i>, a false friend.</p>

<section data-mw-section-id=""20"" id=""mwBRY"" aria-labelledby=""Clock_time""><div class=""mw-heading mw-heading4""><h4 id=""Clock_time"">Clock time</h4><span class=""mw-editsection""><span class=""mw-editsection-bracket"">[</span><a href=""/w/index.php?title=French_phrasebook&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20"" title=""Edit section: Clock time""><span>edit</span></a><span class=""mw-editsection-bracket"">]</span></span></div>
<p id=""mwBRc"">French speakers most commonly use the 24-hour clock, even in Quebec (whereas most other Canadians use the 12-hour clock). In Europe, an 'h' is used as a separator between hours and minutes, as opposed to the colon that is used in Quebec and English-speaking countries. Therefore, midnight is written as <i id=""mwBRg"">0h00</i>, 1AM as <i id=""mwBRk"">1h00</i>, and 1PM as <i id=""mwBRo"">13h00</i>; more details and examples below. However, the 12-hour clock is making some inroads in speech, and saying 1-11 in the afternoon or evening will be understood.</p>

<dl id=""mwBRs""><dt id=""mwBRw"">What's the time?</dt><dd id=""mwBR0"">Quelle heure est-il<span typeof=""mw:DisplaySpace"" id=""mwBR4""> </span>? (<i id=""mwBR8"">kel euhr et-EEL?</i>);</dd>
<dt id=""mwBSA"">hour</dt><dd id=""mwBSE"">heure (<i id=""mwBSI"">eur</i>)</dd>
<dt id=""mwBSM"">minute</dt><dd id=""mwBSQ"">minute (<i id=""mwBSU"">mee-NUUT</i>)</dd>
<dt id=""mwBSY"">From 1 minute past to 30 minutes past the hour</dt><dd id=""mwBSc"">[hour] + [number of minutes]</dd>
<dd id=""mwBSg"">Example: 10<span typeof=""mw:Nowiki"" id=""mwBSk"">:</span>20 or ""twenty past ten"" = <span typeof=""mw:Nowiki"" id=""mwBSo"">10h20</span>; ""dix heures vingt"" (<i id=""mwBSs"">deez eur va(n)</i>)</dd>
<dt id=""mwBSw"">For 31 minutes past to 59 minutes past the hour</dt><dd id=""mwBS0"">[next hour] + moins (<i id=""mwBS4"">mwa(n)</i>)</dd>
<dd id=""mwBS8"">Example: 10<span typeof=""mw:Nowiki"" id=""mwBTA"">:</span>40 or ""twenty to eleven"" = <span typeof=""mw:Nowiki"" id=""mwBTE"">10h40</span>; ""onze heures moins vingt"" (<i id=""mwBTI"">onz eur mwa(n) va(n)</i>)</dd>
<dt id=""mwBTM"">quarter past</dt><dd id=""mwBTQ"">[hour] et quart (<i id=""mwBTU"">ay kahr</i>)</dd>
<dd id=""mwBTY"">Example: 07<span typeof=""mw:Nowiki"" id=""mwBTc"">:</span>15 or ""quarter past seven"" = <span typeof=""mw:Nowiki"" id=""mwBTg"">7h15</span>; ""sept heures et quart"" (<i id=""mwBTk"">set eur eh kahr</i>)</dd>
<dt id=""mwBTo"">quarter to</dt><dd id=""mwBTs"">[hour] moins le quart (<i id=""mwBTw"">mwa(n) luh kahr</i>)</dd>
<dd id=""mwBT0"">Example: 16<span typeof=""mw:Nowiki"" id=""mwBT4"">:</span>45 or ""quarter to five"" = <span typeof=""mw:Nowiki"" id=""mwBT8"">16h45</span>; ""dix sept heures moins le quart"" (<i id=""mwBUA"">dee-set eur mwan luh kahr</i>)</dd>
<dt id=""mwBUE"">half-past</dt><dd id=""mwBUI"">et demie (<i id=""mwBUM"">eh duh-MEE</i>); et demi (after midnight or noon, <i id=""mwBUQ"">eh duh-MEE</i>)</dd>
<dd id=""mwBUU"">Example<span typeof=""mw:DisplaySpace"" id=""mwBUY""> </span>: 10<span typeof=""mw:Nowiki"" id=""mwBUc"">:</span>30 or ""half past ten"" = <span typeof=""mw:Nowiki"" id=""mwBUg"">10h30</span>; ""dix heures et demie"" (<i id=""mwBUk"">deez eur eh duh-MEE</i>)</dd>
<dd id=""mwBUo"">Example<span typeof=""mw:DisplaySpace"" id=""mwBUs""> </span>: 12<span typeof=""mw:Nowiki"" id=""mwBUw"">:</span>30 or ""half past twelve"" = <span typeof=""mw:Nowiki"" id=""mwBU0"">12h30</span>; ""douze heures et demi"" (<i id=""mwBU4"">dooz eur eh duh-MEE</i>)</dd></dl>

<dl id=""mwBU8""><dt id=""mwBVA"">1AM, 01<span typeof=""mw:Nowiki"" id=""mwBVE"">:</span>00</dt><dd id=""mwBVI""><span typeof=""mw:Nowiki"" id=""mwBVM"">1h00</span>; une heure du matin (<i id=""mwBVQ"">uun eur duu ma-TAN</i>)</dd>
<dt id=""mwBVU"">2AM, 02<span typeof=""mw:Nowiki"" id=""mwBVY"">:</span>00</dt><dd id=""mwBVc""><span typeof=""mw:Nowiki"" id=""mwBVg"">2h00</span>; deux heures du matin (<i id=""mwBVk"">dooz eur duu ma-TAN</i>)</dd>
<dt id=""mwBVo"">noon, 12<span typeof=""mw:Nowiki"" id=""mwBVs"">:</span>00</dt><dd id=""mwBVw""><span typeof=""mw:Nowiki"" id=""mwBV0"">12h00</span>; midi (<i id=""mwBV4"">mee-DEE</i>)</dd>
<dt id=""mwBV8"">1PM, 13<span typeof=""mw:Nowiki"" id=""mwBWA"">:</span>00</dt><dd id=""mwBWE""><span typeof=""mw:Nowiki"" id=""mwBWI"">13h00</span>; treize heures (<i id=""mwBWM"">traiyz er</i>)</dd>
<dd id=""mwBWQ"">une heure de l'après-midi (<i id=""mwBWU"">uun eur duh la-preh-mee-DEE</i>)</dd>
<dt id=""mwBWY"">2PM, 14<span typeof=""mw:Nowiki"" id=""mwBWc"">:</span>00</dt><dd id=""mwBWg""><span typeof=""mw:Nowiki"" id=""mwBWk"">14h00</span>; quatorze heures (<i id=""mwBWo"">KAH-torz er</i>)</dd>
<dd id=""mwBWs"">deux heures de l'après-midi (<i id=""mwBWw"">duz er duh la-preh-mee-DEE</i>)</dd>
<dt id=""mwBW0"">6PM, 18<span typeof=""mw:Nowiki"" id=""mwBW4"">:</span>00</dt><dd id=""mwBW8""><span typeof=""mw:Nowiki"" id=""mwBXA"">18h00</span>; dix-huit heures (<i id=""mwBXE"">deez-weet ER</i>)</dd>
<dd id=""mwBXI"">six heures du soir (<i id=""mwBXM"">seez er duu SWAR</i>)</dd>
<dt id=""mwBXQ"">7<span typeof=""mw:Nowiki"" id=""mwBXU"">:</span>30PM, 19<span typeof=""mw:Nowiki"" id=""mwBXY"">:</span>30</dt><dd id=""mwBXc""><span typeof=""mw:Nowiki"" id=""mwBXg"">19h30</span>; dix-neuf heures trente (<i id=""mwBXk"">DEE-znuf er TRAHNT</i>)</dd>
<dd id=""mwBXo"">sept heures et demie (<i id=""mwBXs"">SET er eh duh-MEE</i>)</dd>
<dt id=""mwBXw"">midnight, 00<span typeof=""mw:Nowiki"" id=""mwBX0"">:</span>00</dt><dd id=""mwBX4""><span typeof=""mw:Nowiki"" id=""mwBX8"">0h00</span>; minuit (<i id=""mwBYA"">mee-NWEE</i>)</dd></dl>

<div style=""font-weight:bold;"">Quelle cuisson<span typeof=""mw:DisplaySpace""> </span>?</div>

<p>A common question when ordering meat (especially, but not only, steak) is how long you want it cooked for: rare, medium, or well done? Simple enough, you might think. But if you're from an English-speaking country, then as a general rule of thumb, you'll find that if you ask for what you're used to at home, the meat will be rarer than you'd like. Therefore, it's worth getting to know these terms:</p>
<ul><li><b>Bleu</b> – ""Blue"", almost raw, meat that is cooked for less than a minute each side.</li>
<li><b>Saignant</b> – ""Bloody"", i.e. very rare, but cooked slightly longer than a <i>bleu</i> steak.</li>
<li><b>À point</b> – ""Perfectly cooked"", and the most popular among the French, but still rare by British or American standards.</li>
<li><b>Bien cuit</b> – ""Well cooked"", but not well done. More like medium, with pink on the inside, though there should be no blood.</li>
<li><b>Très bien cuit</b> – This should get you a ""well done"" steak that is totally cooked through. <i>Mais, attention<span typeof=""mw:DisplaySpace""> </span>!</i> If chef is not used to catering to <i>les Anglo-Saxons</i>, he might just overdo it and give you a plate of leather.</li></ul></td></tr>
