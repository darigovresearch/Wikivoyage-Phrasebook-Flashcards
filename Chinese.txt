<p id=""mwFQ""><b id=""mwFg"">Mandarin Chinese</b> is the official language of mainland <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/China"" title=""China"" id=""mwFw"">China</a>, and one of the official languages of <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Taiwan"" title=""Taiwan"" id=""mwGA"">Taiwan</a> and <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Singapore"" title=""Singapore"" id=""mwGQ"">Singapore</a>. Chinese is also one of the official languages in <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Hong_Kong"" title=""Hong Kong"" id=""mwGg"">Hong Kong</a> and <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Macau"" title=""Macau"" id=""mwGw"">Macau</a>, although most local people speak <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Cantonese"" title=""Cantonese"" class=""mw-redirect"" id=""mwHA"">Cantonese</a> instead of Mandarin. In English, it is often just called ""Mandarin"" or ""Chinese"". While not an official language, it is also widely studied and spoken by the Chinese minorities in <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Malaysia"" title=""Malaysia"" id=""mwHQ"">Malaysia</a>, <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Brunei"" title=""Brunei"" id=""mwHg"">Brunei</a> and <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Myanmar"" title=""Myanmar"" id=""mwHw"">Myanmar</a>, and commonly heard in Chinatowns throughout the world. In China, it is called <i id=""mwIA"">Pǔtōnghuà</i> (普通话), meaning ""common speech"", while in Taiwan it is referred to as <i id=""mwIQ"">Guóyǔ</i> (國語), ""the national language."" In Indonesia, it is widely known as <i id=""mwIg"">Zhōnghuáyǔ</i> (中华语), and in Singapore and Malaysia, it simply referred to as <i id=""mwIw"">Huáyǔ</i> (华语). It has been the main language of education in mainland China and Taiwan since the 1950s, so most non-elderly locals speak it regardless of what their native language or dialect is.</p>

<p id=""mwJA"">While the spoken Mandarin in the above places is more or less the same, the written characters are different. Taiwan, Hong Kong and Macau all still use traditional characters, whereas Mainland China and Singapore use a simplified derivative. In Indonesia, the older Chinese generations tend to use the traditional characters, but younger generations tend to use the simplified one, though most of the time, the usage of the Latin script is way more commonly practiced.</p>

<p id=""mwKw"">China is host to a wide variety of related languages (often referred to as dialects), of which Standard Mandarin is just one. Within the Chinese language family, there are 7-10 major branches, each of which contain their own varieties of languages. Languages from different branches (such as Mandarin and <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Cantonese_phrasebook"" title=""Cantonese phrasebook"" id=""mwLA"">Cantonese</a>) are completely mutually unintelligible, whereas languages within the same branch (such as Standard Mandarin and Sichuanese) may have limited mutual intelligibility.</p>

<p id=""mwLQ"">Despite the wide variance in Chinese languages, all speakers normally write the same standard form (using either traditional or simplified characters). This is possible because the Chinese writing system is logographic, meaning individual characters represent ideas as opposed to phonetic sounds. What this means is that one character that would be pronounced completely differently in any number of Chinese languages will be written identically and understood to mean the same thing. Therefore speakers of different Chinese languages who are completely unable to understand each others' speech can effectively communicate via writing. The challenge with a logographic writing system, however, is the huge number of characters required to adequately represent different words: the average Chinese dictionary indexes around 20,000 characters, with an educated Chinese person likely knowing around 8,000, while a typical newspaper requires the reader to know at least 3,000 characters.</p>

<p id=""mwLg"">Historically, there was no standard spoken Chinese, though China's first emperor Qin Shihuang established a literary standard in the 3rd century B.C., which we today call Classical Chinese (文言文 <i id=""mwLw"">wényán wén</i>). Classical Chinese was also adopted as the literary language in Japan, Korea and Vietnam, and was only abandoned in favour of their respective native languages with the rise of nationalism and colonialism in the 19th and 20th centuries. Classical Chinese was notoriously difficult as it had diverged significantly from any variety of vernacular Chinese, and for most of Chinese history, literacy was largely a privilege reserved for the rich elites. In the wake of what was perceived as a humiliation of China in the Treaty of Versailles after <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/World_War_I"" title=""World War I"" id=""mwMA"">World War I</a>, reforms pushed by protesters in the May Fourth Movement led to the establishment of Standard Mandarin as the first standardized spoken form of Chinese for the entire country, as well as a new literary standard based on Standard Mandarin in an effort to increase literacy rates. </p>

<div style=""font-weight:bold;"">A (related) tale from Chinese Wikipedia</div>

<p>In the early days of Chinese Wikipedia, radical supporters of both traditional and simplified Chinese characters vandalized the site by converting the opposing writing system into their favored one. The problem was essentially resolved by creating an automatic translator that converts between traditional and Chinese characters (including vocabulary differences between multiple regions), allowing users to read and edit an article in whichever variety they wish.</p>

<p>It's not a perfect system, as there are about 100 characters that don't have a one-to-one mapping, and tens of thousands of words that need to be translated differently depending on context. And there are still issues that can't be fixed with software, such as writing from a neutral global point of view. But it broadly succeeded at solving the vandalism problem, consolidated Chinese Wikipedia into a single version rather than separate ones for China and Taiwan, and paved the way for automatic translators for other languages that can be written in multiple scripts.</p></td></tr>
</tbody></table>

<p id=""mwMg"">Following the establishment of the People's Republic of China (PRC), formal simplifications were made to a large number of common characters in order to reduce the number of strokes required to write them, with the aim of increasing literacy. This has led to two current standards for Chinese writing: Simplified and Traditional characters. Simplified characters are the standard for Singapore and mainland China, whereas Traditional characters are retained as the standard in Hong Kong, Macau, and Taiwan. The use of traditional or simplified characters can have controversial political connotations, particularly in Hong Kong. Traditional characters are generally preferred in calligraphy, even in mainland China, due to their superior aesthetic value and as a way of honoring the history of the art.</p>

<p id=""mwMw"">About one fifth of the world's population speak some form of Chinese as their native language. It is a tonal language that is related to Burmese and Tibetan. In addition, the Dungan language, which is spoken in some parts of the former <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Soviet_Union"" title=""Soviet Union"" id=""mwNA"">Soviet Union</a>, is considered to be a variant of Mandarin but uses the Cyrillic alphabet instead of Chinese characters.</p>

<p id=""mwNQ"">The writing system is used by other countries as well, although the languages are not related. The <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Korean_phrasebook"" title=""Korean phrasebook"" id=""mwNg"">Korean</a> writing system historically used Chinese characters, but completely abandoned them in favor of its own 'hangul' alphabet since the mid 20th century. South Koreans still learn the basics of Chinese characters, and some basic Chinese characters are still occasionally used and widely understood; <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Japanese_phrasebook"" title=""Japanese phrasebook"" id=""mwNw"">Japanese</a> uses a mixed writing system comprising of Chinese characters and its own 'kana' syllabary, although over time the meaning of some characters has diverged significantly from those used in Chinese: see this <a rel=""mw:WikiLink/Interwiki"" href=""https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Appendix:False%20friends%20between%20Chinese%20and%20Japanese"" title=""wikt:Appendix:False friends between Chinese and Japanese"" class=""extiw"" id=""mwOA"">Wiktionary page</a> for a list of similar words with different meanings in Chinese and Japanese language. The <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Vietnamese_phrasebook"" title=""Vietnamese phrasebook"" id=""mwOQ"">Vietnamese</a> language (which uses a distinctive version of the Latin alphabet) has borrowed many words from Chinese, and prior to the French colonisation used Chinese and Chinese-like characters as well.</p>

<p id=""mwOg"">Standard Mandarin is based on the Mandarin dialect of the <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Beijing"" title=""Beijing"" id=""mwOw"">Beijing</a> area, and is almost universally understood and spoken (in conjunction with local languages) across mainland China and Taiwan as a result of being the primary language of education and the media. Travelers headed to the special administrative regions (SARs) of <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Hong_Kong"" title=""Hong Kong"" id=""mwPA"">Hong Kong</a> or <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Macau"" title=""Macau"" id=""mwPQ"">Macau</a> will largely encounter native <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Cantonese_phrasebook"" title=""Cantonese phrasebook"" id=""mwPg"">Cantonese</a> speakers. Mandarin is largely understood in the SARs, though speaking ability varies widely, and in Hong Kong, the use of Mandarin is a touchy political issue. Those heading for <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Taiwan"" title=""Taiwan"" id=""mwPw"">Taiwan</a> or <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/South_Fujian"" title=""South Fujian"" id=""mwQA"">South Fujian</a> may find the <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Minnan_phrasebook"" title=""Minnan phrasebook"" id=""mwQQ"">Minnan</a> dialect useful as well. However, formal written Chinese in Hong Kong and Macau is still based on Standard Mandarin, though colloquial Cantonese may still be written down in informal settings such as tabloids and entertainment magazines.</p>

<section data-mw-section-id=""2"" id=""mwQg"" aria-labelledby=""Difficulties""><div class=""mw-heading mw-heading3""><h3 id=""Difficulties"">Difficulties</h3><span class=""mw-editsection""><span class=""mw-editsection-bracket"">[</span><a href=""/w/index.php?title=Chinese_phrasebook&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2"" title=""Edit section: Difficulties""><span>edit</span></a><span class=""mw-editsection-bracket"">]</span></span></div>
<p id=""mwQw"">In the West, Chinese has a reputation for difficulty. In fact, its difficulty to learn once led to calls to replace Chinese characters and grammar with Latin script and English grammar during the 1920s by some intellectuals and writers. These opinions eventually died out, but they certainly influenced the ruling communist party's policy on simplified Chinese characters.</p>

<p id=""mwRA"">The language is more dense than European languages, meaning considerably more can be said in a text message with the same number of characters. Each character corresponds to a syllable, and each syllable can have multiple meanings depending on the tone with which it is pronounced. Compared to, say, Japanese or Korean, Chinese contains many fewer loan words from European languages such as English, meaning that more effort will have to be made to acquire vocabulary. The grammar, however, may strike a Westerner as pretty simple. Verbs and adjectives are static regardless of subject and whether they are referring to the past, present or future. Nouns do not have genders like most European languages, and there is no separate form for plurals or separate cases. The main difficulties are the five tones, numerous consonants that are not found in European languages, and the numerous characters.</p>

<p id=""mwRQ"">Mandarin, like Vietnamese and Thai, is a tonal language that uses different pitches in syllables and words to indicate different meanings. ""Ma"" could mean mother, horse, numb, or blame, depending on the tone. Homophones are also common; the same sound at the same pitch can have dozens of meanings. ""Zhōng"" (""Zhong"" with the 1st tone) can mean China/central/centre (中), loyalty (忠), clock (钟), chime (钟), finish (终), a bowl (盅), etc. All of them come with different Chinese characters, just the same sound at the same pitch. While homophones are rarely a problem in most everyday conversations, it is common for Chinese to ask how to write someone's name by identifying the characters one by one. ""My name is Wang Fei (王菲). Wang is the ""wang"" with four strokes, Fei is the ""fei"" in ""shifei"" (gossip), with the grass <a rel=""mw:WikiLink/Interwiki"" href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese%20character%20radicals"" title=""w:Chinese character radicals"" class=""extiw"" id=""mwRg"">radical</a> on top.""</p>

<p id=""mwRw"">Written Chinese looks like a mysterious secret code to some, but if you can recognize so many commercial logos (usually not logically related), you will be impressed with your capacity to memorize so many characters - most of which are logically related and formed based on certain rules.</p>

<p id=""mwSA"">There are, in theory, more than 50,000 Chinese characters. The good news is that more than 85% have become obsolete, or are rarely used. Like native speakers of many languages, most Chinese couldn't tell you how many characters are required to read a book and never bother to count how many characters they know. One may argue that junior students are supposed to learn at least 2000 characters and university graduates 5000 characters.</p>

<p id=""mwTw"">To bridge the gap between recognizing characters and reading out loud, Hanyu pinyin was developed, which uses Latin script as an aid to teaching Chinese. Pronouncing pinyin is not intuitive as certain letters and consonant clusters are used to represent sounds not present in European languages and are thus not pronounced as an English speaker would expect. Nonetheless, learning pinyin at even a basic level has enormous practical value for the traveller. Written pinyin is less useful as most Chinese will not recognize place names or addresses in pinyin, and the same pinyin can be shared by different Chinese characters; it is always better to use characters for written communication.</p>

<p id=""mwUA"">The pronunciation guide below uses Hanyu pinyin, which is the official romanization of the <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/People's_Republic_of_China"" title=""People's Republic of China"" class=""mw-redirect"" id=""mwUQ"">People's Republic of China</a> and <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Taiwan"" title=""Taiwan"" id=""mwUg"">Taiwan</a>. Mainland China uses Hanyu pinyin almost universally, though a few older transliterations remain in names like <i id=""mwUw"">Tsingtao Beer</i> and <i id=""mwVA"">Peking University</i>. <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Taiwan"" title=""Taiwan"" id=""mwVQ"">Taiwan</a> used to use the <a rel=""mw:WikiLink/Interwiki"" href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wade-Giles"" title=""w:Wade-Giles"" class=""extiw"" id=""mwVg"">Wade-Giles</a> system, which is quite different, then in 2002 switched to <a rel=""mw:WikiLink/Interwiki"" href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tongyong_Pinyin"" title=""w:Tongyong Pinyin"" class=""extiw"" id=""mwVw"">Tongyong pinyin</a>, only slightly different from Hanyu pinyin, and since 2009 uses Hanyu pinyin just like the People's Republic. However, many older transliterations remain in use in Taiwan, and you may well encounter multiple spellings of the same name (like <i id=""mwWA"">Tamsui</i>, <i id=""mwWQ"">Tamshui</i>, and <i id=""mwWg"">Danshui</i> for <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Tamsui"" title=""Tamsui"" id=""mwWw"">the town in New Taipei</a>).</p>

<p id=""mwXA"">Pinyin allows very accurate pronunciation of Mandarin for those who understand it, although the way that it uses letters like <i id=""mwXQ"">q</i>, <i id=""mwXg"">x</i>, <i id=""mwXw"">c</i>, <i id=""mwYA"">z</i> and even <i id=""mwYQ"">i</i> is not at all intuitive to the English speaker since some of these sounds do not exist in English or many other languages. Thus, studying the pronunciation guide below carefully is essential. After you master the pronunciation you will need to move on to the next challenge: using accurate tones whilst speaking.</p>

<p id=""mwYg"">Some pinyin vowels (especially ""e"", ""i"", ""ü"") can be tricky, so it is best to get a native speaker to demonstrate. Also, beware of the spelling rules listed in the <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""#exceptions"" class=""mw-selflink-fragment"" id=""mwYw"">exceptions</a> below.</p>

<dt id=""mwaA"">a in ian and yan</dt><dd id=""mwaQ"">as ""e"" in ""b<b id=""mwag"">e</b>t"" or ""t<b id=""mwaw"">e</b>xt"" (just the English short ""e"" sound)</dd>
<dt id=""mwbA"">e</dt><dd id=""mwbQ"">unrounded back vowel (IPA [ɤ]), similar to d<b id=""mwbg"">uh</b>; in unstressed syllables, a schwa (IPA [ə]), like ide<b id=""mwbw"">a</b></dd>
<dt id=""mwcA"">i</dt><dd id=""mwcQ"">as in s<b id=""mwcg"">ee</b> or k<b id=""mwcw"">ey</b>;<br id=""mwdA""/>after <i id=""mwdQ"">ch</i>, <i id=""mwdg"">sh</i>, <i id=""mwdw"">zh</i>, <i id=""mweA"">c</i>, <i id=""mweQ"">s</i>, <i id=""mweg"">z</i> or <i id=""mwew"">r</i>, not really a vowel at all but just a stretched-out consonant sound</dd>

</section><section data-mw-section-id=""5"" id=""mwuQ"" aria-labelledby=""Consonants""><div class=""mw-heading mw-heading3""><h3 id=""Consonants"">Consonants</h3><span class=""mw-editsection""><span class=""mw-editsection-bracket"">[</span><a href=""/w/index.php?title=Chinese_phrasebook&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5"" title=""Edit section: Consonants""><span>edit</span></a><span class=""mw-editsection-bracket"">]</span></span></div>
<p id=""mwug"">Chinese stops distinguish between <i id=""mwuw"">aspirated</i> and <i id=""mwvA"">unaspirated</i>, not <i id=""mwvQ"">unvoiced</i> and <i id=""mwvg"">voiced</i> as in English, and Chinese lacks voiced stops. Aspirated sounds are pronounced with a distinctive puff of air as they are pronounced in English when at the beginning of a word, while unaspirated sounds are pronounced without the puff, as in English when found in clusters.</p>

<p id=""mwvw"">Place a hand in front of your mouth and compare <b id=""mwwA"">p</b>it (aspirated) with s<b id=""mwwQ"">p</b>it (unaspirated) to see the difference. Note that the list below only gives approximate pronunciations, as many of these consonants have no equivalent in English.  As Mandarin does not have voiced stops or affricates, all the consonants listed in the ""unaspirated"" column should be pronounced unvoiced.</p>

<p id=""mwATk"">If you think that is a fairly intimidating repertoire, rest assured that many Chinese people, particularly those who are not native Mandarin speakers, will merge many of the sounds above (especially <i id=""mwATo"">c</i> with <i id=""mwATs"">ch</i> and <i id=""mwATw"">z</i> with <i id=""mwAT0"">zh</i>). These are particularly common in southern China, Taiwan and overseas Chinese communities.</p>

<div style=""font-weight:bold;"">How do I put my tone marks?</div>

<p>If you are confused by how to put tone marks above the Hanyu Pinyin, follow the steps below:</p>

<p>Always insert tone marks above the vowels. If there is more than one vowel letter, follow the steps below:</p>

<p>(1) Insert it above the 'a' if that letter is present. For example, it is <i>r<b>ǎ</b>o</i> and not <i>ra<b>ǒ</b></i></p>

<p>(2) If not, insert it above 'o'. For example, <i>gu<b>ó</b></i> and not <i>g<b>ú</b>o</i></p>

<p>(3) Insert it above the letter 'e' if the letters 'a' and 'o' are not present. For example, <i>ju<b>é</b></i> and not <i>j<b>ú</b>e</i></p>

<p>(4) If only 'i', 'u' and 'ü' are the only present letters, insert it in the letter that occurs <b>last</b>. For example, <i>ji<b>ù</b></i> and not <i>j<b>ì</b>u</i>, <i>chu<b>í</b></i> and not <i>ch<b>ú</b>i</i>. Note, if the vowel present is ü, the tone mark is put <b>in addition</b> to the umlaut. For example, l<b>ǜ</b></p></td></tr>

</tbody></table>
<p id=""mwAV8"">There are five tones in Mandarin that must be followed for proper pronunciation. Never underestimate the importance of these tones. Consider a vowel with a different tone as simply a different vowel altogether, and you will realize why Chinese will <i id=""mwAWA"">not</i> understand you if you use the wrong tone <span typeof=""mw:Entity"" id=""mwAWE"">—</span> <i id=""mwAWI"">mǎ</i> is to <i id=""mwAWM"">mā</i> as ""I want a cake"" is to ""I want a coke"".  Be especially wary of questions that have a falling tone, or conversely exclamations that have an ""asking"" tone (eg <i id=""mwAWQ"">jǐngchá</i>, police). In other words, <i id=""mwAWU"">pronounced like</i> does not imply <i id=""mwAWY"">meaning</i>. While Mandarin speakers also vary their tone just like English speakers do to differentiate a statement from a question and convey emotion, it is much more subtle. Do not try it until you have mastered the basic tones.</p>

<dl id=""mwAWc""><dt id=""mwAWg"">1. first tone ( ā )</dt><dd id=""mwAWk"">""high tone"": flat, high pitch that is more sung instead of spoken.</dd>
<dt id=""mwAWo"">2. second tone ( á )</dt><dd id=""mwAWs"">""rising tone"": low to middle, rising pitch that is pronounced like the end of a question phrase (<i id=""mwAWw"">Whát?</i>).</dd>
<dt id=""mwAW0"">3. third tone ( ǎ )</dt><dd id=""mwAW4"">""low tone"" or ""falling-rising"": mid-low to low. For two consecutive syllables in the third tone, the first syllable is pronounced as if it is in the second tone. For example, 打扰 <i id=""mwAW8"">dǎrǎo</i> is pronounced as <i id=""mwAXA"">dárǎo</i>. (When stressed, the third tone is sometimes pronounced mid-low to low to high, dipping pitch.)</dd>
<dt id=""mwAXE"">4. fourth tone ( à )</dt><dd id=""mwAXI"">""falling tone"": high to low, rapidly falling pitch that is pronounced like a command (<i id=""mwAXM"">Stop!</i>).</dd>
<dt id=""mwAXQ"">5. neutral tone ( a )</dt><dd id=""mwAXU"">""toneless"": short, unstressed syllable; rarely used by itself (except for phrase particles) but frequently occurs as the second part of a phrase.</dd></dl>

<p id=""mwAf0"">Also note that while the term 小姐 (小姐) <i id=""mwAf4"">xiǎo jiě</i> is often used to address young women and waitresses in Taiwan (and in Singapore and Malaysia), it is a euphemism for ""prostitute"" in mainland China, and thus would be considered an insult. In mainland China, the corresponding word for addressing a young woman would be 女士 (女士) <i id=""mwAf8"">nǚ shì</i> (which can be used for women of all ages), while the word for addressing a waitress would be 服务员 (服務員) <i id=""mwAgA"">fú wù yuán</i> (which is used for waitstaff of both sexes). Similarly, while 台妹 (臺妹) <i id=""mwAgE"">tái mèi</i> is used in mainland China to refer to any young Taiwanese woman, in Taiwan it is a derogatory term referring to an uncultured young woman from the countryside.</p>

<p id=""mwAgI"">Another term that can cause confusion is 冲凉 (沖涼) <i id=""mwAgM"">chōng liáng</i>, which means ""to take a cold shower"" in China, but refers to taking any kind of shower in Malaysia and Singapore. Similarly 做工 (做工) <i id=""mwAgQ"">zuò gōng</i> means ""to work"", but it refers specifically to doing manual labour in mainland China and Taiwan, while it refers to doing any kind of work in Malaysia and Singapore.</p>

<div style=""font-weight:bold;"">Pronouns</div>

<p>Chinese pronouns are relatively straightforward.</p>
<ul><li>我 <i>wǒ</i> is the standard first person pronoun, while 你 <i>nǐ</i> is the standard second person pronoun, though 您 <i>nín</i> is a more respectful pronoun that should be used when addressing people of higher status, and is also sometimes used by shop assistants to address customers.</li>
<li>The equivalent of the third-person pronouns ""he"", ""she"" and ""it"" are 他, 她 and 牠/它 respectively, all pronounced <i>tā</i>. In traditional Chinese, a distinction is made between 牠 for referring to animals, and 它 for referring to inanimate objects, but in simplified Chinese only 它 is used. 祂 is used in Christian texts to refer to God.</li>
<li>Plurals are relatively straightforward, and are formed by adding a 们 (們) <i>men</i> behind the singular pronoun, so 我们 (我們) <i>wǒmen</i> means ""we"", 你们 (你們) <i>nǐmen</i> is the equivalent of the plural ""you"" and 他们 (他們) <i>tāmen</i> means ""they"".</li>
<li>In Northern China, 咱们 (咱們) <i>zámen</i> is used as the inclusive ""we"" (i.e. if the subject includes the person(s) you are talking to), and 我们 (我們) is used as the exclusive ""we"" (i.e. if the subject does not include the person(s) you are talking to), though this distinction is not made in southern China, Taiwan, Malaysia and Singapore, which only use 我们 (我們).</li></ul></td></tr>

<div style=""font-weight:bold;"">To be or not to be?</div>

<p>Chinese does not have words for ""yes"" and ""no"" as such; instead, questions are typically answered by repeating the verb. Here are common examples:</p>
<dl><dt>To be or not to be</dt><dd>是 (是) <i>shì</i>, 不是 (不是) <i>bú shì</i></dd>
<dt>To have or not have / there is or is not</dt><dd>有 (有) <i>yǒu</i>, 没有 (没有) <i>méi yǒu</i></dd>
<dt>To be right or wrong</dt><dd>对 (對) <i>duì</i>, 不对 (不對) <i>bú duì</i></dd></dl>
<p>If all this seems like too much to handle, you can say ""yes"" with a short falling-tone grunt (嗯 <i>ǹg</i>), and you'll usually be understood if you say 不 <i>bù</i> for ""no"".</p></td></tr>

<div style=""font-weight:bold;"">Number gestures</div>

<p>Chinese people use a set of gestures for the numbers 1-10. They're a useful way to communicate prices and quantities, especially if you're having trouble understanding or pronouncing the Chinese word for a number. The gestures vary a bit by region.</p>

<div style=""font-weight:bold;""><i>Shí kuài</i> or <i>sì kuài</i>?</div>

<p>In southern China, Taiwan, Malaysia and Singapore, many speakers don't distinguish the <i>sh</i> and <i>s</i> sounds, which means that in these areas, the only reliable difference between <i>shí</i> (ten) and <i>sì</i> (four) is the tone. So if you're talking to someone from southern China, it's important to listen for the tone when they tell you a number, lest you think something costs 4 yuan when it's really 10.</p></td></tr>
</tbody></table>
<p id=""mwBJU"">For numbers above 100, any ""gaps"" must be filled in with 零 <i id=""mwBJY"">líng</i>, as e.g. 一百一 <i id=""mwBJc"">yībǎiyī</i> would otherwise be taken as shorthand for ""110"". A single unit of tens may be written and pronounced either 一十 <i id=""mwBJg"">yīshí</i> or just 十 <i id=""mwBJk"">shí</i>.</p>

<dt id=""mwBJ4"">101</dt><dd id=""mwBJ8"">一百零一 (一百零一) <i id=""mwBKA"">yī-bǎi-líng-yī</i></dd>
<dt id=""mwBKE"">110</dt><dd id=""mwBKI"">一百一十 (一百一十) <i id=""mwBKM"">yī-bǎi-yī-shí</i></dd>

<p id=""mwBLg"">Numbers starting from 10,000 are grouped by in units of four digits starting with 万 (萬) <i id=""mwBLk"">wàn</i> (ten thousand). ""One million"" in Chinese is thus ""hundred ten-thousands"" 一百万 (一百萬), and ""one billion"" is ""ten hundred-millions"" 十亿 (十億).</p>

<dt id=""mwBME"">10,002</dt><dd id=""mwBMI"">一万零二 (一萬零二) <i id=""mwBMM"">yī-wàn-líng-èr</i></dd>
<dt id=""mwBMQ"">20,000</dt><dd id=""mwBMU"">二万 (二萬) <i id=""mwBMY"">èr-wàn</i> (written) or 两万 (兩萬) <i id=""mwBMc"">liǎng-wàn</i> (colloquial)</dd>

<p id=""mwBOo"">Measure words are used in combination with a number to indicate an amount of something, similar to how English requires ""two <i id=""mwBOs"">pieces of</i> furniture"" rather than just ""two furniture"". However, unlike in English, Chinese requires counter words for <i id=""mwBOw"">all</i> nouns, whether countable or uncountable.</p>

<p id=""mwBO0"">When unsure, use 个 (個) <i id=""mwBO4"">ge</i>; even though it may not be correct, you will probably be understood because it is the generic and most common measure word.</p>

<p id=""mwBcU"">Weekdays in Chinese are easy: starting with 1 for Monday, just add the number after 星期 (星期) <i id=""mwBcY"">xīngqī</i>. In <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Taiwan"" title=""Taiwan"" id=""mwBcc"">Taiwan</a> and Singapore, 星期 (星期) is pronounced <i id=""mwBcg"">xīngqí</i> (second tone on the second syllable).</p>

<dl id=""mwBck""><dt id=""mwBco"">Sunday</dt><dd id=""mwBcs"">星期天 (星期天) <i id=""mwBcw"">xīngqītiān</i> / 星期日 (星期日) <i id=""mwBc0"">xīngqīrì</i></dd>

<dt id=""mwBdc"">Thursday</dt><dd id=""mwBdg"">星期四 (星期四) <i id=""mwBdk"">xīngqīsì</i></dd>
<dt id=""mwBdo"">Friday</dt><dd id=""mwBds"">星期五 (星期五) <i id=""mwBdw"">xīngqīwǔ</i></dd>

<p id=""mwBeA"">In colloquial usage, 星期 (星期) can also be replaced with 礼拜 (禮拜) <i id=""mwBeE"">lǐbài</i> or just 拜 (拜) <i id=""mwBeI"">bài</i>, and 周 (週) <i id=""mwBeM"">zhōu</i>, but only 礼拜天 (禮拜天) <i id=""mwBeQ"">lǐbàitiān</i> and 周日 (週日) <i id=""mwBeU"">zhōurì</i> are used, while 礼拜日 (禮拜日) or 周天 (週天) are not used. 礼拜 (禮拜) <i id=""mwBeY"">lǐbài</i> on its own without a suffix can also be used to refer to Sunday. A common way to refer to Saturday and Sunday collectively is 拜六礼拜 (拜六禮拜) <i id=""mwBec"">bài lìu lǐ bài</i>.</p>

</section><section data-mw-section-id=""21"" id=""mwBhU"" aria-labelledby=""Writing_dates""><div class=""mw-heading mw-heading4""><h4 id=""Writing_dates"">Writing dates</h4><span class=""mw-editsection""><span class=""mw-editsection-bracket"">[</span><a href=""/w/index.php?title=Chinese_phrasebook&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21"" title=""Edit section: Writing dates""><span>edit</span></a><span class=""mw-editsection-bracket"">]</span></span></div>

<tbody><tr><td>
<div style=""font-weight:bold;"">Writing dates in the lunar calendar</div>

<p>If you are attempting to name a date in the Chinese lunar calendar, add the words 农历 (農曆) before the name of the month to distinguish it from the months of the solar calendar, although it is not strictly necessary. There are some differences: The words 日(日) <i>rì</i>/ 号(號) <i>hào</i> are generally not required when stating dates in the lunar calendar; it is assumed. Besides that, the 1st Month is called 正月 (正月) <i>zhēngyuè</i>. If the number of the day is less than 11, the word 初 (初) is used before the value of the day. Besides that, if the value of the day is more than 20, the word 廿 (廿) <i>niàn</i> is used, so the 23rd day is 廿三 (廿三) for example.</p></td></tr>
</tbody></table>
<p id=""mwBhc"">In Chinese, years of the Gregorian calendars are generally read as individual digits. For instance, the year 1945 (the last year of World War II) is read as <i id=""mwBhg"">yī jǐu sì wǔ nián</i>. The exception is the last year of each millennium, an example being the year 2000, which is read <i id=""mwBhk"">liǎng qiān nián</i> as if it were an ordinary number.</p>

<dl id=""mwBho""><dt id=""mwBhs"">15th day of the 8th lunar month (the mid-autumn festival)</dt><dd id=""mwBhw"">(农历)八月十五 ((農曆)八月十五) <i id=""mwBh0"">(nónglì) bāyuè shí-wǔ</i>.</dd>
<dt id=""mwBh4"">1st day of the 1st lunar month</dt><dd id=""mwBh8"">(农历)正月初一 ((農曆)正月初一) <i id=""mwBiA"">(nónglì) zhèngyuè chūyī</i>.</dd>
<dt id=""mwBiE"">23rd day of the 9th lunar month</dt><dd id=""mwBiI"">(农历)九月廿三 ((農曆)九月廿三) <i id=""mwBiM"">(nónglì) jiŭ yuè niànsān</i>.  }}</dd></dl>
<p id=""mwBiQ"">When writing the date, you name the month <i id=""mwBiU"">(number (1-12) + 月 (月) </i>yuè<i id=""mwBiY"">)</i>, before inserting the day <i id=""mwBic"">(number (1-31) + 日(日) </i>rì<i id=""mwBig"">/ 号(號) </i>hào<i id=""mwBik"">)</i>. Note that the usage of 号(號) <i id=""mwBio"">hào</i> is more colloquial than that of 日(日) <i id=""mwBis"">rì</i>, the latter of which is used in writing and formal speech.</p>

<dl id=""mwBiw""><dt id=""mwBi0"">6th January</dt><dd id=""mwBi4"">一月六号 (一月六號) <i id=""mwBi8"">yī yuè liù hào</i> or 一月六日 (一月六日) <i id=""mwBjA"">yī yuè liù rì</i></dd>
<dt id=""mwBjE"">25th December</dt><dd id=""mwBjI"">十二月二十五号 (十二月二十五號) <i id=""mwBjM"">shí-èr yuè èr-shí-wǔ hào</i></dd></dl>

<p id=""mwBjQ"">In <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Taiwan"" title=""Taiwan"" id=""mwBjU"">Taiwan</a>, <b id=""mwBjY""><a rel=""mw:WikiLink/Interwiki"" href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Republic%20of%20China%20calendar"" title=""w:Republic of China calendar"" class=""extiw"" id=""mwBjc"">Minguo calendar</a></b> (民國紀年/民国纪年) is also frequently used. 2023 in the Gregorian calendar corresponds to <i id=""mwBjg"">Minguo 112 nian</i> (民國112年/民国112年). If you see some dates like ""112/12/25"" (Minguo 112, December 25) in Taiwan, they are probably using the Minguo calendar to address the year. To convert Minguo year into the Gregorian calendar, add 1911 (The last year before the Republic of China was established) in Minguo year. Therefore, <i id=""mwBjk"">Minguo 100 nian</i> (民國100年/民国100年) is 2012 in the Gregorian calendar. Years before 1912 (i.e. You will get zero or a negative number when subtracting 1911) will add the ""前"" prefix before the year. So <i id=""mwBjo"">Minguo qian 2 nian</i> (民國前2年/民国前2年) is 1910 in the Gregorian calendar.</p>

<p id=""mwBxQ"">The base unit of currency in Chinese is the 元 (<i id=""mwBxU"">yuán</i>), which could be translated as ""dollar"", or simply transliterated as ""yuan"", depending on what currency you are referring to. In financial contexts, such as when writing cheques and printing banknotes, 元 is written as 圆 (圓). One yuan is divided into 10 角 (<i id=""mwBxY"">jiǎo</i>), which is in turn divided into 10 分 (<i id=""mwBxc"">fēn</i>). Colloquially, the yuan is often referred to as the 块 (塊) (<i id=""mwBxg"">kuài</i>), while the jiao is referred to as the 毛 (<i id=""mwBxk"">máo</i>).</p>

<p id=""mwCHg"">Bargaining (还价 (還價) <i id=""mwCHk"">huán jià</i>) is possible (and expected) in markets and many small shops. The first price you are given will usually be hugely inflated - it's up to you to <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Haggle"" title=""Haggle"" class=""mw-redirect"" id=""mwCHo"">haggle</a> it down to something more acceptable. This will probably feel awkward if you aren't used to it, and you may worry about ending up cheating the seller. Don't worry - sellers won't take a price that's too low, and you will usually end up agreeing on a price that's considerably lower than the starting one but still allows the seller to make a profit. Try starting at around 20-30% of the original price; you can always work up from there. The exceptions to the rule are supermarkets, large department stores, bookshops and some of the higher end boutiques, most of which will have signs letting you know that haggling is unacceptable in these stores.</p>

</section></section><section data-mw-section-id=""36"" id=""mwCW4"" aria-labelledby=""Learning_more""><div class=""mw-heading mw-heading2""><h2 id=""Learning_more"">Learning more</h2><span class=""mw-editsection""><span class=""mw-editsection-bracket"">[</span><a href=""/w/index.php?title=Chinese_phrasebook&amp;action=edit&amp;section=36"" title=""Edit section: Learning more""><span>edit</span></a><span class=""mw-editsection-bracket"">]</span></span></div>
<p id=""mwCW8"">Chinese language learning is flourishing as foreigners recognize the importance of gaining the ability to effectively communicate with a population of 1.3+ billion people. Due to the rapid rise of the Chinese teaching industry however, finding consistent quality instruction can be difficult. Many Chinese language schools and institutes have opened up over the past decade both abroad as well as within China, though before enrolling it is definitely recommended to thoroughly research and speak with current or former students to ensure that effective education is being provided.</p>

<p id=""mwCXA"">For independent learners, it is imperative that one first master tones and the pinyin system before beginning to build vocabulary. The <a rel=""mw:ExtLink nofollow"" href=""https://www.chinesetest.cn/HSK"" class=""external text"" id=""mwCXE"">Hanyu Shuiping Kaoshi</a> (HSK, 汉语水平考试) or Chinese Proficiency Test is China's standardized test for Chinese language proficiency (equivalent to the English TOEFL or IELTS). The HSK provides detailed guides for essential vocabulary and grammatical concepts in increasing level of fluency up to Level 9, which represents full written and oral abilities in the language. A good idea for practicing is to make Chinese friends online since millions of young people in China also look for somebody to practice English with. In Taiwan, the equivalent of the HSK is the <a rel=""mw:ExtLink nofollow"" href=""https://tocfl.edu.tw/"" class=""external text"" id=""mwCXI"">Test of Chinese as a Foreign Language</a> (TOCFL,  華語文能力測驗), though it is not as widely available or recognised internationally as the HSK.</p>

<ul id=""mwCXM""><li id=""mwCXQ""><a rel=""mw:ExtLink"" href=""https://en.wikibooks.org/wiki/Chinese_%28Mandarin%29"" class=""external text"" data-mw-original-href=""http://en.wikibooks.org/wiki/Chinese_%28Mandarin%29"" id=""mwCXU"">Chinese (Wikibooks.org)</a>: Free lessons providing detailed grammar explanations, audio samples and stroke order animations.</li>
<li id=""mwCXY""><a rel=""mw:ExtLink nofollow"" href=""http://www.digitaldialects.com/Chinese.htm"" class=""external text"" id=""mwCXc"">Digital Dialects Chinese</a>: Interactive games for learning Chinese in both Pinyin and simplified characters.</li>
<li id=""mwCXg""><a rel=""mw:ExtLink nofollow"" href=""http://www.chinese-course.com"" class=""external text"" id=""mwCXk"">Chinese Flashcards</a>: Annotated Texts, Flashcards, Multiple choice tests</li>
<li id=""mwCXo""><a rel=""mw:ExtLink nofollow"" href=""https://deardimsum.wordpress.com/"" class=""external text"" id=""mwCXs"">Dear Dim Sum, daily small bites Chinese lessons</a></li>
<li id=""mwCXw""><a rel=""mw:ExtLink nofollow"" href=""http://chinesereadingpractice.com/"" class=""external text"" id=""mwCX0"">Chinese Reading Practice</a>: Annotated stories for learners at varying levels</li></ul>

<p id=""mwCX4"">English-Chinese and Chinese-English dictionaries are often disappointingly incomplete and have surprisingly many errors, at least once you get past the basics. Some decent digital dictionaries include:</p>

<ul id=""mwCX8""><li id=""mwCYA"">Pleco (possibly the most complete Chinese-English dictionary app aimed at learners)</li>
<li id=""mwCYE"">Youdao (有道词典, popular with Chinese students learning English)</li>
<li id=""mwCYI""><a rel=""mw:WikiLink/Interwiki"" href=""https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/"" title=""wikt:"" class=""extiw"" id=""mwCYM"">Wiktionary</a></li></ul>

<p id=""mwCYQ"">The first two can be downloaded as apps, and Pleco can be used offline.</p>
