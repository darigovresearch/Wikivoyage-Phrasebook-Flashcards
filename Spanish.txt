"Introduction (Wikivoyage Phrasebook Spanish)"|"
<pThis deck has taken words outlined in the Spanish Phrasebook of the <a href=""https://en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Phrasebooks"">Wikivoyage Phrasebook</a> pages and put them into a form which can be easily learnt/revised using <a href=""https://apps.ankiweb.net/"">Anki</a> a cross platform app specifically designed for long term knowledge retention.</p>
<h2>Notes</h2>
<p>Please note the modifications which have been made &amp; where you can find updates.</p>
<ol align=""left"">
<li>All the vocabulary from each language has been arranged in a way that can be easily learnt from Anki, separated into decks for each language</li>
<li>Any updates, corrections or translations to the deck will be available at <a href=""https://github.com/darigovresearch/Wikivoyage-Phrasebook-Flashcards""></a><a href=""https://github.com/darigovresearch/Wikivoyage-Phrasebook-Flashcards"">https://github.com/darigovresearch/Wikivoyage-Phrasebook-Flashcards</a> so do return periodically to check if you have the latest version.</li>
<li>Modifications from the original work includes typo correction, card merging &amp; consistency consolidation (see the commit history for for the specific changes)</li>
</ol>
<p>Feel free to share the deck and give the repository a star so more people are likely to see this work and can get the most out of it.</p>
<h2>Usage</h2>
<p>If you want to just learn the words, download <a href=""https://apps.ankiweb.net/"">Anki</a> on your device of choice and open the .apkg file to add it into your collection. You can choose to download the whole texbook as a single deck or you can download it a language at a time from the individual language files in the ""APKG"" folder for the relevant language. If you wish to build the deck from scratch you will need to import the .txt file via the desktop with HTML enabled and the separator to be ""|"".</p>

What should you know about Spanish?|"<p id=""mwCQ""><b id=""mwCg"">Spanish</b> (<i id=""mwCw"">español</i>), also known as <b id=""mwDA"">Castilian</b> (<i id=""mwDQ"">castellano</i>), is the third most-spoken language in the world (after English and <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Chinese_phrasebook"" title=""Chinese phrasebook"" id=""mwDg"">Mandarin Chinese</a>), with around 540<span typeof=""mw:Entity"" id=""mwDw"">&nbsp;</span>million speakers. Originating in <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Spain"" title=""Spain"" id=""mwEA"">Spain</a> and spoken by most residents there, it is also an official language in <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Mexico"" title=""Mexico"" id=""mwEQ"">Mexico</a> and all of <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Central_America"" title=""Central America"" id=""mwEg"">Central America</a> except Belize (though it is widely understood there as well), and of most countries in <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/South_America"" title=""South America"" id=""mwEw"">South America</a>: <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Argentina"" title=""Argentina"" id=""mwFA"">Argentina</a>, <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Bolivia"" title=""Bolivia"" id=""mwFQ"">Bolivia</a>, <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Chile"" title=""Chile"" id=""mwFg"">Chile</a>, <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Colombia"" title=""Colombia"" id=""mwFw"">Colombia</a>, <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Ecuador"" title=""Ecuador"" id=""mwGA"">Ecuador</a>, <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Paraguay"" title=""Paraguay"" id=""mwGQ"">Paraguay</a>, <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Peru"" title=""Peru"" id=""mwGg"">Peru</a>, <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Uruguay"" title=""Uruguay"" id=""mwGw"">Uruguay</a>, <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Venezuela"" title=""Venezuela"" id=""mwHA"">Venezuela</a>. What should you know about Spanish with respect to Romance languages?|"<p id=""mwMg"">A Western Romance language, Spanish is closely related to and somewhat mutually intelligible with the other Romance languages, such as <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Catalan_phrasebook"" title=""Catalan phrasebook"" id=""mwMw"">Catalan</a>, <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Italian_phrasebook"" title=""Italian phrasebook"" id=""mwNA"">Italian</a>, <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Portuguese_phrasebook"" title=""Portuguese phrasebook"" id=""mwNQ"">Portuguese</a>, and <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Romanian_phrasebook"" title=""Romanian phrasebook"" id=""mwNg"">Romanian</a>. English and Spanish share variants of approximately one third of their words (via Latin), although the pronunciation and spelling tend to be very different.</p>

What should you be careful with respect to Spanish?|"<p id=""mwNw"">Be careful of false friends if you speak Portuguese or Italian. Examples of these include Portuguese <i id=""mwOA"">embaraçada</i> (embarrassed) vs Spanish <i id=""mwOQ"">embarazada</i> (pregnant), Portuguese <i id=""mwOg"">oficina</i> (workshop) vs Spanish <i id=""mwOw"">oficina</i> (office), Italian <i id=""mwPA"">burro</i> (butter) vs Spanish <i id=""mwPQ"">burro</i> (donkey) and Italian <i id=""mwPg"">carta</i> (paper) vs Spanish <i id=""mwPw"">carta</i> (letter).</p>

What should you know about Grammatical gender in Spanish?|"<p id=""mwQw"">Spanish nouns come in two genders, feminine and masculine. Grammatical gender is not tied to biological sex, so unlike English, even inanimate objects are assigned a gender (e.g. <i id=""mwRA"">silla</i> [chair] is feminine; <i id=""mwRQ"">libro</i> [book] is masculine). The article before the noun depends on the gender and number: the definite article (which in English is <i id=""mwRg"">the</i>) is <i id=""mwRw"">la</i> for singular feminine nouns, <i id=""mwSA"">el</i> for singular masculine nouns, <i id=""mwSQ"">las</i> for plural feminine nouns, and <i id=""mwSg"">los</i> for plural masculine nouns. The grammatical gender of nouns referring to people generally follow their sex, but there are nouns which remain of the same gender regardless of the sex of the person they are referring to (e.g. <i id=""mwSw"">persona</i> [person] is feminine even if referring to a male person). While this may sound confusing or disorientating to English speakers at first, the system can be understood quickly. Most nouns that end in <i id=""mwTA"">-o</i> are masculine, and most nouns that end in <i id=""mwTQ"">-a</i> are feminine. Most nouns ending in consonants are also masculine, with some notable exceptions. For instance, every noun ending in <i id=""mwTg"">-ción</i> (e.g. <i id=""mwTw"">informa<b id=""mwUA"">ción</b></i> [information], <i id=""mwUQ"">na<b id=""mwUg"">ción</b></i> [nation]) is feminine. If you make a gender-based mistake (e.g. saying <i id=""mwUw""><b id=""mwVA"">el</b> mesa</i> rather than <i id=""mwVQ""><b id=""mwVg"">la</b> mesa</i> [the table]), Spanish speakers will still understand you, although you may be corrected. There are some common words which break these conventions (e.g. <i id=""mwVw""><b id=""mwWA"">la</b> man<b id=""mwWQ"">o</b></i> [the hand]) or which can even take either gender depending on dialect (e.g. <i id=""mwWg"">la/el mar</i> [the sea]).</p>

<p id=""mwWw"">Third person pronouns also differ based on grammatical gender: <i id=""mwXA"">él</i> (""he"") for masculine nouns and <i id=""mwXQ"">ella</i> (""she"") for feminine nouns, with the corresponding plurals being <i id=""mwXg"">ellos</i> and <i id=""mwXw"">ellas</i> respectively. There is no equivalent of ""it"" in Spanish.</p>
"
What should you know about Verb tenses in Spanish?|"<p id=""mwYQ"">The Spanish verb tense system is fairly similar to English, but all six person/number combinations take different endings in the indicative. The <b id=""mwYg"">formal you</b> (<i id=""mwYw"">usted</i> in singular, <i id=""mwZA"">ustedes</i> in plural) takes a third-person verb. Adjectives must match the gender and number of the noun they're describing, so a man says <i id=""mwZQ"">encantad<b id=""mwZg"">o</b></i> and a woman says <i id=""mwZw"">encantad<b id=""mwaA"">a</b></i> for being ""delighted"" on meeting you. Adjectives always inflect to match the gender and plurality, even if the noun has the ""wrong""-looking ending; for example, ""clean hands"" is <i id=""mwaQ"">manos limpi<b id=""mwag"">a</b>s</i>, even though <i id=""mwaw"">mano</i> ends in an <i id=""mwbA"">-o</i>. The indirect object and the animate direct object are both marked by <i id=""mwbQ""><b id=""mwbg"">a</b></i>: <i id=""mwbw"">preguntar a alguien</i> (to ask somebody).</p>
"
What should you do when in doubt with respect to Spanish grammar?|"<p id=""mwcQ"">The Spanish institution in charge of the language is the ""<a rel=""mw:ExtLink nofollow"" href=""https://www.rae.es/"" class=""external text"" id=""mwcg"">RAE</a>"" (often pronounced as a word rather than the individual letters) the Real Academia Española or Royal Spanish Academy of the language founded in the 18th century with the perhaps somewhat antiquated motto to ""clean, make certain and give splendor"" to the language. While their linguistic prescriptivism may rub some the wrong way and despite efforts to include more Latin American voices it is still a largely eurocentric institution, one can be certain that any use endorsed by the RAE will be acceptable in all corners of the Spanish speaking world if in some cases a bit stilted. Still, the reluctance of the RAE to embrace change, particularly Anglophone loanwords or efforts at gender neutral language, has made it lose a bit of its influence even though it is still seen as the ""gold standard"" of the language in Spain, it is much less influential in Latin America where people tend to speak (and to some extent write) without giving heed to the linguistic commandments from Madrid.</p>
"
What should you know about pronunciation in Spanish?|"<p id=""mwdA"">Spanish spelling has the pleasant characteristic of being very phonetic, with only a few clearly-defined exceptions. This means that if you know how to pronounce the letters of a word, it's relatively easy to sound out the word itself. Although Spanish has loan words that have been acquired from a variety of other languages, it is nothing like the hodge-podge that is English, with wildly unpredictable spelling, etymology, and grammatical rules which cannot be relied upon. Spanish also has a much stronger tendency to ""localize"" loanwords than English, which means spelling, pronunciation or both will be changed to better fit the logic of the Spanish language, resulting in words like ""beisbol"".</p>

<p id=""mwdQ"">Spanish pronunciation varies by region, though speakers from different countries can generally understand each other without trouble. Dialects are usually grouped into the varieties spoken in Spain (which is called Iberian, Peninsular, or European Spanish) and the varieties spoken in Latin America, but there is substantial variation between each of these groups. The one most striking difference between <i id=""mwdg"">all</i> Latin American dialects and <i id=""mwdw"">most</i> Spanish dialects lies in the pronunciation of ""z"", and ""c"" before ""e"" or ""i"". While all Latin American dialects produce an ""s"" sound (thus making ""caza"" and ""casa"" homophones) most Spanish dialects in Spain produce a ""th"" sound (with the tongue between the teeth, represented as ""θ"") for those consonants.</p>
"
What should you know about vowels in Spanish?|"
<p id=""mweQ"">The vowels in Spanish are short crisp sounds. They are not dragged out like some English vowels. Spanish makes no distinction between ""long"" and ""short"" vowels and Spanish-speakers are unlikely to even hear a difference. There is however a distinction between stressed and unstressed.</p>

A soft vibration sound almost like English 'v' elsewhere, specifically a 'v' sound but with your two lips pressed together in a 'b' or 'p' shape with the airflow of a 'v'.</dd>"

When followed by 'e' or 'i', it is like 's' in ""supper"" (in the Americas, the Canaries and some parts of the Philippines) or 'th' in ""thin"" (Spain): <i id=""mwjw"">cine</i> (cinema, pronounced by Latin Americans as <i id=""mwkA"">SEE-nay</i>, Spaniards as <i id=""mwkQ"">THEE-nay</i>)</dd>"
<dt id=""mwkg"">ch</dt>|"<dd id=""mwkw"">like 'ch' in ""touch"": <i id=""mwlA"">muchacho</i> (boy) [tʃ]</dd>"

<dt id=""mwmQ"">f</dt>|"<dd id=""mwmg"">like 'f' in ""fine"": <i id=""mwmw"">faro</i> (lighthouse)</dd>"

In the clusters ""gue"" and ""gui"", the 'u' serves only to change the sound of the consonant and is silent (<i id=""mwoA"">guitarra</i>, guitar), unless it bears a diaeresis, as in ""güe"" and ""güi"" (<i id=""mwoQ"">pedigüeño</i>, beggar). <dt id=""mwpQ"">j</dt>|"<dd id=""mwpg"">like a throaty 'h' in ""ha"": <i id=""mwpw"">jamón</i> (ham), the sound of Scottish or German ""ch"" as in ""Loch"" is close [x]</dd>"

<dt id=""mwrw"">ll</dt>|"<dd id=""mwsA"">Varies substantially by dialect. The easiest widely understandable pronunciation is like 'y' in ""year"": <i id=""mwsQ"">llamar</i> (to call). Pronounced like a 'zh' [ʒ] as in 'Zhivago' or 'sh' [ʃ] as in 'show' only in Argentina and Uruguay; in some other countries it may be pronounced like an English 'j' [dʒ] as in ""ninja"" or like 'ly' [ʎ] as in the English word ""million"". <dt id=""mwtQ"">n</dt>|"<dd id=""mwtg"">like 'n' in ""nice"", and like 'n' in ""anchor"": <i id=""mwtw"">noche</i> (night), <i id=""mwuA"">ancla</i> (anchor)</dd>"
<dt id=""mwuQ"">ñ</dt>|"<dd id=""mwug"">like 'ny' in ""canyon"": <i id=""mwuw"">cañón</i> (canyon) [ɳ], <i id=""mwvA"">piñata</i>. This is a separate letter in the Spanish alphabet. Pronouncing this as ""n"" will usually be intelligible but can sometimes make an entirely separate word. For instance, <i id=""mwvQ"">año</i> is ""year"" but <i id=""mwvg"">ano</i> is ""anus""—you'll want to avoid saying that you have 33 anuses when you mean to say that you're 33 years old.</dd>"
<dt id=""mwvw"">p</dt>|"<dd id=""mwwA"">like 'p' in ""spit"": <i id=""mwwQ"">perro</i> (dog)</dd>"
<dt id=""mwwg"">q</dt>|"<dd id=""mwww"">like 'k' in ""ski"" (always with a silent ""u""): <i id=""mwxA"">queso</i>, pronounced <i id=""mwxQ"">KAY-so</i> (cheese)</dd>"
<dt id=""mwxg"">r, rr</dt>|"<dd id=""mwxw"">Spanish has two 'r' sounds, both of which are different from their counterpart in English. Some effort should be made to approximate each of them, to help listeners distinguish between <i id=""mwyA"">perro</i> (""dog"") and <i id=""mwyQ"">pero</i> (""but"") — or perhaps to understand you at all:
<ul id=""mwyg""><li id=""mwyw"">single <b id=""mwzA"">r</b>: For American English speakers, like the <i id=""mwzQ"">dd</i> in ""ladder"". This sound is created by putting the tip of the tongue up against where the front of the roof of the mouth meets the upper teeth, very similar to the action English speakers make to pronounce <i id=""mwzg"">l</i> or <i id=""mwzw"">d</i>. To some English speakers, it may sound a bit like a combined ""d-r"". Take care to pronounce <i id=""mw0A"">r</i> separately when it follows a consonant; a blended English <i id=""mw0Q"">tr</i> will not be recognized in the Spanish word <i id=""mw0g"">otro</i> (""other""), which should be pronounced more like ""OHT-roh"". Try to avoid the common pitfall to distinguish the words by the vowel: There is no difference in the e sound of pero and perro and Spanish native speakers won't hear any if you try to make one.</li>
<li id=""mw0w"">rolled <b id=""mw1A"">r</b>: Written ""r"" at the beginning of the word, or ""rr"" between vowels. It's a trill, a multiply vibrating sound. Whereas most English speakers can learn to tap out a single <i id=""mw1Q"">r</i>, many adults learning Spanish find this sound impossible to produce; in this case, pronouncing it like a Spanish <i id=""mw1g"">r</i> or fumbling out a <i id=""mw1w"">d-r</i> will be better understood than pronouncing it like a long English <i id=""mw2A"">r</i>.</li></ul></dd>"
<dt id=""mw2Q"">s</dt>|"<dd id=""mw2g"">like 's' in ""son"": <i id=""mw2w"">sopa</i> (soup); in Spain, it is often pronounced like a soft, palatised ""sh"" at the end of a word or syllable.</dd>"
<dt id=""mw3A"">t</dt>|"<dd id=""mw3Q"">like 't' in ""stop"": <i id=""mw3g"">tapa</i> (top)</dd>"
<dt id=""mw3w"">v</dt>|"<dd id=""mw4A"">Pronounced the same way as <i id=""mw4Q"">b</i> (see above): like 'b' in ""elbow"" at the beginning of a word and after 'n'; closer to the English 'v' sound elsewhere. What should you know about Castilian and Latin American Spanish?|"
<p id=""mwAYc"">In addition to pronunciation differences, there are significant lexical differences between the Spanish spoken in Spain and the Spanish spoken in Latin America. There are also often lexical differences between different Latin American Spanish dialects (e.g. pineapple is <i id=""mwAYg"">piña</i> in Mexico and <i id=""mwAYk"">ananá</i> in Argentina). In particular, the Spanish spoken in Argentina and Uruguay has strong <a rel=""mw:WikiLink"" href=""//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Italian_phrasebook"" title=""Italian phrasebook"" id=""mwAYo"">Italian</a> influences, and some items may be better known by their Italian names instead of their standard Spanish names (e.g. <i id=""mwAYs"">birra</i> instead of <i id=""mwAYw"">cerveza</i> for beer).</p>

<p id=""mwAY0"">To overgeneralize, the higher the register of language (think highbrow newspapers or government communiques) the more similar American and Peninsular Spanish will be. However, ""slang"" and spoken language can diverge significantly, drawing reactions ranging from puerile snickering to baffled looks to simply not being understood when trying to use the wrong slang in the wrong country.</p>

What should you know about América in Spanish?|"<p>In Spanish, the term <i>América</i> typically refers to the entire American continent from Canada down to Chile, while the United States of America is properly referred to as <i>los Estados Unidos</i>. Saying ""<i>Soy americano.</i>"" (literally ""I am an American."") to mean that you're from the United States is fairly common but may nonetheless earn you a lecture on the meaning of ""America"", so you should instead say ""<i>Soy estadounidense.</i>"" (literally ""I am a United Statesian"").</p>

<p>Be cautious about how you say that you're from a particular state:  A <i>Californio</i> is a person whose family was in California while that land was still part of a Spanish colony, a true <i>Tejano</i> may never have set foot&nbsp;in the modern US state of Texas, and the family of a <i>Neomexicano</i> was speaking Spanish in the 17th and 18th centuries.  Try ""Soy de California"" (or Texas, or Nuevo México) instead.</p>

What should you know about numbers in Spanish?|<p id=""mwApU"">In general, the Spanish numbering system is fairly straightforward. For numbers from 21–29, the final ""e"" of ""veinte"" is substituted with an ""i"", and the second digit is added to the back. For numbers from 31–99, the tens and ones are separated by ""y"" (e.g. 31: treinta y uno; 99: noventa y nueve). The ""y"" is not used to separate the hundreds from the tens, or the thousands from the hundreds. Numbers from 101 to 199 are named by using <i id=""mwApY"">ciento</i> + <i id=""mwApc"">cardinal number</i>. From 200 onwards, the hundreds are named by using the <i id=""mwApg"">cardinal number</i> + <i id=""mwApk"">cientos</i> (eg: 200: doscientos), but the numbers 500, 700 and 900 are exceptions to this rule and must be memorised. Naming of the thousands is straightforward, with <i id=""mwApo"">cardinal number</i> + <i id=""mwAps"">mil</i>. From the millions onwards, the plural for is used for numbers above 1,000,000. Also, unlike English, Spanish uses the long scale. Therefore, <i id=""mwApw"">un billón</i> and <i id=""mwAp0"">un trillón</i> is not the same as the English ""one billion"" and ""one trillion"".</p>
"What should you know about counting in ""hundreds"" in Spanish?"|"<p id=""mwAp4"">Counting in ""hundreds"" is unheard of for numbers larger than 1 000. This is also true for years. Instead of ""nineteen hundred fifty three"" a Spanish speaker would say ""mil novecientos cincuenta y tres"".</p>"
What should you know about the thousands separator in Spanish?|"<p id=""mwAp8"">Opposite to English, a comma "","" is used as a decimal point when writing numbers in Spanish, while a period ""."" is used to separate groups of thousands. This means that the number written 1,000,000.5 in English will usually be written 1.000.000,5 in Spanish (though you may see 1,000,000.5 in Mexico or the U.S.)</p>"
