<p id="mwCQ"><b id="mwCg">Spanish</b> (<i id="mwCw">español</i>), also known as <b id="mwDA">Castilian</b> (<i id="mwDQ">castellano</i>), is the third most-spoken language in the world (after English and <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Chinese_phrasebook" title="Chinese phrasebook" id="mwDg">Mandarin Chinese</a>), with around 540<span typeof="mw:Entity" id="mwDw">&nbsp;</span>million speakers. Originating in <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Spain" title="Spain" id="mwEA">Spain</a> and spoken by most residents there, it is also an official language in <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico" id="mwEQ">Mexico</a> and all of <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America" id="mwEg">Central America</a> except Belize (though it is widely understood there as well), and of most countries in <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/South_America" title="South America" id="mwEw">South America</a>: <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina" id="mwFA">Argentina</a>, <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Bolivia" title="Bolivia" id="mwFQ">Bolivia</a>, <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Chile" title="Chile" id="mwFg">Chile</a>, <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia" id="mwFw">Colombia</a>, <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Ecuador" title="Ecuador" id="mwGA">Ecuador</a>, <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Paraguay" title="Paraguay" id="mwGQ">Paraguay</a>, <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Peru" title="Peru" id="mwGg">Peru</a>, <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Uruguay" title="Uruguay" id="mwGw">Uruguay</a>, <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Venezuela" title="Venezuela" id="mwHA">Venezuela</a>. Spanish as a second language is generally understood to some level in most of the rest of the continent.</p>

They are mostly concentrated in Europe and the Americas.</figcaption></figure>

<p id="mwIQ">In the <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean" id="mwIg">Caribbean</a>, Spanish is spoken in <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba" id="mwIw">Cuba</a>, the <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Dominican_Republic" title="Dominican Republic" id="mwJA">Dominican Republic</a>, and <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico" id="mwJQ">Puerto Rico</a>. In the <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/United_States" title="United States" id="mwJg">United States</a>, Spanish is a co-official language of <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/New_Mexico" title="New Mexico" id="mwJw">New Mexico</a>, and is also a first language for many people especially in <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/California" title="California" id="mwKA">California</a>, <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Texas" title="Texas" id="mwKQ">Texas</a>, <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City" id="mwKg">New York City</a>, <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago" id="mwKw">Chicago</a>, <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/South_Florida" title="South Florida" id="mwLA">South Florida</a>, and most of the <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Southwest_(United_States_of_America)" title="Southwest (United States of America)" class="mw-redirect" id="mwLQ">American Southwest</a>. There are around 50<span typeof="mw:Entity" id="mwLg">&nbsp;</span>million Spanish speakers (including native and second language speakers) in the U.S., making it the country with the second-highest Spanish-speaking population in the world after Mexico. Additionally, Spanish is an official language in the African country of <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Equatorial_Guinea" title="Equatorial Guinea" id="mwLw">Equatorial Guinea</a>. In Europe, it is a widely spoken unofficial language in <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Andorra" title="Andorra" id="mwMA">Andorra</a> and in the British territory of <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Gibraltar" title="Gibraltar" id="mwMQ">Gibraltar</a>.</p>

<p id="mwMg">A Western Romance language, Spanish is closely related to and somewhat mutually intelligible with the other Romance languages, such as <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Catalan_phrasebook" title="Catalan phrasebook" id="mwMw">Catalan</a>, <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Italian_phrasebook" title="Italian phrasebook" id="mwNA">Italian</a>, <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Portuguese_phrasebook" title="Portuguese phrasebook" id="mwNQ">Portuguese</a>, and <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Romanian_phrasebook" title="Romanian phrasebook" id="mwNg">Romanian</a>. English and Spanish share variants of approximately one third of their words (via Latin), although the pronunciation and spelling tend to be very different.</p>

<p id="mwNw">Be careful of false friends if you speak Portuguese or Italian. Examples of these include Portuguese <i id="mwOA">embaraçada</i> (embarrassed) vs Spanish <i id="mwOQ">embarazada</i> (pregnant), Portuguese <i id="mwOg">oficina</i> (workshop) vs Spanish <i id="mwOw">oficina</i> (office), Italian <i id="mwPA">burro</i> (butter) vs Spanish <i id="mwPQ">burro</i> (donkey) and Italian <i id="mwPg">carta</i> (paper) vs Spanish <i id="mwPw">carta</i> (letter).</p>

<section data-mw-section-id="2" id="mwQg" aria-labelledby="Grammatical_gender"><div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Grammatical_gender">Grammatical gender</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="//en.wikivoyage.org/w/index.php?title=Spanish_phrasebook&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Grammatical gender"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p id="mwQw">Spanish nouns come in two genders, feminine and masculine. Grammatical gender is not tied to biological sex, so unlike English, even inanimate objects are assigned a gender (e.g. <i id="mwRA">silla</i> [chair] is feminine; <i id="mwRQ">libro</i> [book] is masculine). The article before the noun depends on the gender and number: the definite article (which in English is <i id="mwRg">the</i>) is <i id="mwRw">la</i> for singular feminine nouns, <i id="mwSA">el</i> for singular masculine nouns, <i id="mwSQ">las</i> for plural feminine nouns, and <i id="mwSg">los</i> for plural masculine nouns. The grammatical gender of nouns referring to people generally follow their sex, but there are nouns which remain of the same gender regardless of the sex of the person they are referring to (e.g. <i id="mwSw">persona</i> [person] is feminine even if referring to a male person). While this may sound confusing or disorientating to English speakers at first, the system can be understood quickly. Most nouns that end in <i id="mwTA">-o</i> are masculine, and most nouns that end in <i id="mwTQ">-a</i> are feminine. Most nouns ending in consonants are also masculine, with some notable exceptions. For instance, every noun ending in <i id="mwTg">-ción</i> (e.g. <i id="mwTw">informa<b id="mwUA">ción</b></i> [information], <i id="mwUQ">na<b id="mwUg">ción</b></i> [nation]) is feminine. If you make a gender-based mistake (e.g. saying <i id="mwUw"><b id="mwVA">el</b> mesa</i> rather than <i id="mwVQ"><b id="mwVg">la</b> mesa</i> [the table]), Spanish speakers will still understand you, although you may be corrected. There are some common words which break these conventions (e.g. <i id="mwVw"><b id="mwWA">la</b> man<b id="mwWQ">o</b></i> [the hand]) or which can even take either gender depending on dialect (e.g. <i id="mwWg">la/el mar</i> [the sea]).</p>

<p id="mwWw">Third person pronouns also differ based on grammatical gender: <i id="mwXA">él</i> ("he") for masculine nouns and <i id="mwXQ">ella</i> ("she") for feminine nouns, with the corresponding plurals being <i id="mwXg">ellos</i> and <i id="mwXw">ellas</i> respectively. There is no equivalent of "it" in Spanish.</p>

</section><section data-mw-section-id="3" id="mwYA" aria-labelledby="Verb_tense"><div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Verb_tense">Verb tense</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="//en.wikivoyage.org/w/index.php?title=Spanish_phrasebook&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Verb tense"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p id="mwYQ">The Spanish verb tense system is fairly similar to English, but all six person/number combinations take different endings in the indicative. The <b id="mwYg">formal you</b> (<i id="mwYw">usted</i> in singular, <i id="mwZA">ustedes</i> in plural) takes a third-person verb. Adjectives must match the gender and number of the noun they're describing, so a man says <i id="mwZQ">encantad<b id="mwZg">o</b></i> and a woman says <i id="mwZw">encantad<b id="mwaA">a</b></i> for being "delighted" on meeting you. Adjectives always inflect to match the gender and plurality, even if the noun has the "wrong"-looking ending; for example, "clean hands" is <i id="mwaQ">manos limpi<b id="mwag">a</b>s</i>, even though <i id="mwaw">mano</i> ends in an <i id="mwbA">-o</i>. The indirect object and the animate direct object are both marked by <i id="mwbQ"><b id="mwbg">a</b></i>: <i id="mwbw">preguntar a alguien</i> (to ask somebody).</p>

</section><section data-mw-section-id="4" id="mwcA" aria-labelledby="When_in_doubt"><div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="When_in_doubt">When in doubt</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="//en.wikivoyage.org/w/index.php?title=Spanish_phrasebook&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: When in doubt"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p id="mwcQ">The Spanish institution in charge of the language is the "<a rel="mw:ExtLink nofollow" href="https://www.rae.es/" class="external text" id="mwcg">RAE</a>" (often pronounced as a word rather than the individual letters) the Real Academia Española or Royal Spanish Academy of the language founded in the 18th century with the perhaps somewhat antiquated motto to "clean, make certain and give splendor" to the language. While their linguistic prescriptivism may rub some the wrong way and despite efforts to include more Latin American voices it is still a largely eurocentric institution, one can be certain that any use endorsed by the RAE will be acceptable in all corners of the Spanish speaking world if in some cases a bit stilted. Still, the reluctance of the RAE to embrace change, particularly Anglophone loanwords or efforts at gender neutral language, has made it lose a bit of its influence even though it is still seen as the "gold standard" of the language in Spain, it is much less influential in Latin America where people tend to speak (and to some extent write) without giving heed to the linguistic commandments from Madrid.</p>

</section></section><section data-mw-section-id="5" id="mwcw" aria-labelledby="Pronunciation_guide"><div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Pronunciation_guide">Pronunciation guide</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="//en.wikivoyage.org/w/index.php?title=Spanish_phrasebook&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Pronunciation guide"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p id="mwdA">Spanish spelling has the pleasant characteristic of being very phonetic, with only a few clearly-defined exceptions. This means that if you know how to pronounce the letters of a word, it's relatively easy to sound out the word itself. Although Spanish has loan words that have been acquired from a variety of other languages, it is nothing like the hodge-podge that is English, with wildly unpredictable spelling, etymology, and grammatical rules which cannot be relied upon. Spanish also has a much stronger tendency to "localize" loanwords than English, which means spelling, pronunciation or both will be changed to better fit the logic of the Spanish language, resulting in words like "beisbol".</p>

<p id="mwdQ">Spanish pronunciation varies by region, though speakers from different countries can generally understand each other without trouble. Dialects are usually grouped into the varieties spoken in Spain (which is called Iberian, Peninsular, or European Spanish) and the varieties spoken in Latin America, but there is substantial variation between each of these groups. The one most striking difference between <i id="mwdg">all</i> Latin American dialects and <i id="mwdw">most</i> Spanish dialects lies in the pronunciation of "z", and "c" before "e" or "i". While all Latin American dialects produce an "s" sound (thus making "caza" and "casa" homophones) most Spanish dialects in Spain produce a "th" sound (with the tongue between the teeth, represented as "θ") for those consonants.</p>

<section data-mw-section-id="6" id="mweA" aria-labelledby="Vowels"><div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Vowels">Vowels</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="//en.wikivoyage.org/w/index.php?title=Spanish_phrasebook&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Vowels"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p id="mweQ">The vowels in Spanish are short crisp sounds. They are not dragged out like some English vowels. Spanish makes no distinction between "long" and "short" vowels and Spanish-speakers are unlikely to even hear a difference. There is however a distinction between stressed and unstressed.</p>

<dl id="mweg"><dt id="mwew">a</dt><dd id="mwfA">like 'a' in "father"</dd>
<dt id="mwfQ">e</dt><dd id="mwfg">between 'ay' in "pay" and 'e' in "pet"</dd>
<dt id="mwfw">i</dt><dd id="mwgA">like 'ee' in "see"</dd>
<dt id="mwgQ">o</dt><dd id="mwgg">like 'o' in "roll"</dd>
<dt id="mwgw">u</dt><dd id="mwhA">like 'u' in "rule"</dd>
<dt id="mwhQ">y</dt><dd id="mwhg">like 'ee' in "see". Very rarely used at the middle or ending of words. Like "y" in young at the beginning of words.</dd></dl>

</section><section data-mw-section-id="7" id="mwhw" aria-labelledby="Consonants"><div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Consonants">Consonants</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="//en.wikivoyage.org/w/index.php?title=Spanish_phrasebook&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Consonants"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<dl id="mwiA"><dt id="mwiQ">b</dt><dd id="mwig">like 'b' in "elbow" at the beginning of a word and after 'm': <i id="mwiw">boca</i> (mouth). A soft vibration sound almost like English 'v' elsewhere, specifically a 'v' sound but with your two lips pressed together in a 'b' or 'p' shape with the airflow of a 'v'.</dd>
<dt id="mwjA">c</dt><dd id="mwjQ">In most cases it is pronounced like 'c' in "scare": <i id="mwjg">calle</i> (street).  When followed by 'e' or 'i', it is like 's' in "supper" (in the Americas, the Canaries and some parts of the Philippines) or 'th' in "thin" (Spain): <i id="mwjw">cine</i> (cinema, pronounced by Latin Americans as <i id="mwkA">SEE-nay</i>, Spaniards as <i id="mwkQ">THEE-nay</i>)</dd>
<dt id="mwkg">ch</dt><dd id="mwkw">like 'ch' in "touch": <i id="mwlA">muchacho</i> (boy) [tʃ]</dd>
<dt id="mwlQ">d</dt><dd id="mwlg">like 'd' in "dog" at the beginning of a sentence or after 'n' or 'l'; like the 'th' in "the" between vowels: <i id="mwlw">de</i> (of/from), <i id="mwmA">pasado</i> (the adjective or noun past). You're usually fine just using the 'th' sound as in "the".</dd>
<dt id="mwmQ">f</dt><dd id="mwmg">like 'f' in "fine": <i id="mwmw">faro</i> (lighthouse)</dd>
<dt id="mwnA">g</dt><dd id="mwnQ">when followed by 'e' or 'i', like a throaty 'h' (<i id="mwng">general</i> = heh-neh-RAHL, meaning general), otherwise like 'g' in "ago" (<i id="mwnw">gato</i>, cat). In the clusters "gue" and "gui", the 'u' serves only to change the sound of the consonant and is silent (<i id="mwoA">guitarra</i>, guitar), unless it bears a diaeresis, as in "güe" and "güi" (<i id="mwoQ">pedigüeño</i>, beggar). In between vowels, it tends to be weakened to a softer sound, [ɣ].</dd>
<dt id="mwog">h</dt><dd id="mwow">silent: <i id="mwpA">hora</i>= OR-ah (hour). Pronounced like a softer 'j' only in foreign words.</dd>
<dt id="mwpQ">j</dt><dd id="mwpg">like a throaty 'h' in "ha": <i id="mwpw">jamón</i> (ham), the sound of Scottish or German "ch" as in "Loch" is close [x]</dd>
<dt id="mwqA">k</dt><dd id="mwqQ">like 'k' in "ski" or silent like Spanish H: <i id="mwqg">kilo</i> <i id="mwqw">knox</i> The letter K is only used in foreign words (kárate, kilo, Kevin, Karen, etc.).</dd>
<dt id="mwrA">l</dt><dd id="mwrQ">like 'l' in "love": <i id="mwrg">lápiz</i> (pen)</dd>
<dt id="mwrw">ll</dt><dd id="mwsA">Varies substantially by dialect. The easiest widely understandable pronunciation is like 'y' in "year": <i id="mwsQ">llamar</i> (to call). Pronounced like a 'zh' [ʒ] as in 'Zhivago' or 'sh' [ʃ] as in 'show' only in Argentina and Uruguay; in some other countries it may be pronounced like an English 'j' [dʒ] as in "ninja" or like 'ly' [ʎ] as in the English word "million". The [ʎ] pronunciation was once enforced in school education as the "only correct" form especially in Francoist Spain but is now retreating northwards in Spain and otherwise only prevalent among bilingual speakers whose other language (e.g. Catalan or Quechua) contains this phoneme.</dd>
<dt id="mwsg">m</dt><dd id="mwsw">like 'm' in "mother": <i id="mwtA">mano</i> (hand, a rare example of a word whose grammatical gender breaks the rules outlined above—"la mano" is correct)</dd>
<dt id="mwtQ">n</dt><dd id="mwtg">like 'n' in "nice", and like 'n' in "anchor": <i id="mwtw">noche</i> (night), <i id="mwuA">ancla</i> (anchor)</dd>
<dt id="mwuQ">ñ</dt><dd id="mwug">like 'ny' in "canyon": <i id="mwuw">cañón</i> (canyon) [ɳ], <i id="mwvA">piñata</i>. This is a separate letter in the Spanish alphabet. Pronouncing this as "n" will usually be intelligible but can sometimes make an entirely separate word. For instance, <i id="mwvQ">año</i> is "year" but <i id="mwvg">ano</i> is "anus"—you'll want to avoid saying that you have 33 anuses when you mean to say that you're 33 years old.</dd>
<dt id="mwvw">p</dt><dd id="mwwA">like 'p' in "spit": <i id="mwwQ">perro</i> (dog)</dd>
<dt id="mwwg">q</dt><dd id="mwww">like 'k' in "ski" (always with a silent "u"): <i id="mwxA">queso</i>, pronounced <i id="mwxQ">KAY-so</i> (cheese)</dd>
<dt id="mwxg">r, rr</dt><dd id="mwxw">Spanish has two 'r' sounds, both of which are different from their counterpart in English. Some effort should be made to approximate each of them, to help listeners distinguish between <i id="mwyA">perro</i> ("dog") and <i id="mwyQ">pero</i> ("but") — or perhaps to understand you at all:
<ul id="mwyg"><li id="mwyw">single <b id="mwzA">r</b>: For American English speakers, like the <i id="mwzQ">dd</i> in "ladder". This sound is created by putting the tip of the tongue up against where the front of the roof of the mouth meets the upper teeth, very similar to the action English speakers make to pronounce <i id="mwzg">l</i> or <i id="mwzw">d</i>. To some English speakers, it may sound a bit like a combined "d-r". Take care to pronounce <i id="mw0A">r</i> separately when it follows a consonant; a blended English <i id="mw0Q">tr</i> will not be recognized in the Spanish word <i id="mw0g">otro</i> ("other"), which should be pronounced more like "OHT-roh". Try to avoid the common pitfall to distinguish the words by the vowel: There is no difference in the e sound of pero and perro and Spanish native speakers won't hear any if you try to make one.</li>
<li id="mw0w">rolled <b id="mw1A">r</b>: Written "r" at the beginning of the word, or "rr" between vowels. It's a trill, a multiply vibrating sound. Whereas most English speakers can learn to tap out a single <i id="mw1Q">r</i>, many adults learning Spanish find this sound impossible to produce; in this case, pronouncing it like a Spanish <i id="mw1g">r</i> or fumbling out a <i id="mw1w">d-r</i> will be better understood than pronouncing it like a long English <i id="mw2A">r</i>.</li></ul></dd>
<dt id="mw2Q">s</dt><dd id="mw2g">like 's' in "son": <i id="mw2w">sopa</i> (soup); in Spain, it is often pronounced like a soft, palatised "sh" at the end of a word or syllable.</dd>
<dt id="mw3A">t</dt><dd id="mw3Q">like 't' in "stop": <i id="mw3g">tapa</i> (top)</dd>
<dt id="mw3w">v</dt><dd id="mw4A">Pronounced the same way as <i id="mw4Q">b</i> (see above): like 'b' in "elbow" at the beginning of a word and after 'n'; closer to the English 'v' sound elsewhere. To distinguish <i id="mw4g">v</i> from <i id="mw4w">b</i> when saying the name of the letter, one says "ve chica" or "be grande" to indicate which; native Spanish speakers may not hear the difference between "vee" and "bee".</dd>
<dt id="mw5A">w</dt><dd id="mw5Q">like 'w' in "weight" in English words, <i id="mw5g">whisky</i>, (pronounced "WEESS-kee"). Like 'b' in "bed" in German words. This letter is never used in native Spanish words and you can live your entire life in a Hispanic country and never hear it outside of the word "whisky".</dd>
<dt id="mw5w">x</dt><dd id="mw6A">like 'x' in "flexible" (<i id="mw6Q">flexible</i>). Like 'ss' in "hiss" at beginning of a word (<i id="mw6g">xilófono</i>, xylophone). Like a throaty 'h' in the words <i id="mw6w">México, mexicano, Oaxaca,</i> and <i id="mw7A">oaxaqueño</i>. Often found in words of indigenous American origin where it may not follow Spanish pronunciation logic.</dd>
<dt id="mw7Q">y</dt><dd id="mw7g">like 'y' in "yes": <i id="mw7w">payaso</i> (clown). Like 'y' in "boy": <i id="mw8A">hoy</i> (now). Pronounced like a 'zh' [ʒ] as in 'Zhivago' or 'sh' [ʃ] as in 'show' only in Argentina and Uruguay: <i id="mw8Q">yo no sé</i> (I don't know), pronounced "zhoh noh say".</dd>
<dt id="mw8g">z</dt><dd id="mw8w">like 's' in "supper" (Latin America, parts of Spain), like 'th' in "thin" (most of Spain) [θ]: <i id="mw9A">zorro</i> (fox). See <b id="mw9Q">c</b> above.</dd></dl>

<p id="mw9g">Similarities in pronunciation between b/p/v can be difficult for <i id="mw9w">gringos</i> (English speakers). Many Spanish speakers are familiar with Anglo cultures, particularly in Mexico and Spain, where they interact with English-speakers on a regular basis, so they may have more familiarity with your inability to pronounce their words as they do. If you are visiting remote villages in the Andes, Hispanics there will be less likely to decode mispronunciations.</p>

</section><section data-mw-section-id="8" id="mw-A" aria-labelledby="Diphthongs"><div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Diphthongs">Diphthongs</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="//en.wikivoyage.org/w/index.php?title=Spanish_phrasebook&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Diphthongs"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p id="mw-Q">Most diphthongs can be approximated by blending the first vowel into the second in a single syllable. However, according to the Real Academía Española <i id="mw-g">only</i> diphthongs containing an i or u are "one syllable", all others are two syllables (this is relevant in questions of where to place accents)</p>
<dl id="mw-w"><dt id="mw_A">ai, ay</dt><dd id="mw_Q">like 'eye': <i id="mw_g">baile</i> (<i id="mw_w">BAI-leh</i>, dance)</dd>
<dt id="mwAQA">au</dt><dd id="mwAQE">like 'ow' in "cow": <i id="mwAQI">causa</i> (<i id="mwAQM">KOW-sah</i>, cause)</dd>
<dt id="mwAQQ">ea</dt><dd id="mwAQU">like 'eh-ah': <i id="mwAQY">fea</i> (<i id="mwAQc">FEH-ah</i>, ugly)</dd>
<dt id="mwAQg">ei, ey</dt><dd id="mwAQk">like 'ay' in "say": <i id="mwAQo">reina, rey.</i> (<i id="mwAQs">RAY-nah</i>, queen)</dd>
<dt id="mwAQw">eu</dt><dd id="mwAQ0">like 'eh-oo': <i id="mwAQ4">euro</i> ("EH-oo-roh")</dd></dl>

<dl id="mwAQ8"><dt id="mwARA">ia</dt><dd id="mwARE">like 'ee-yah': <i id="mwARI">piano</i> (<i id="mwARM">pee-YAH-noh</i>)</dd>
<dt id="mwARQ">ie</dt><dd id="mwARU">like 'ee-yeh': <i id="mwARY">pie</i> (<i id="mwARc">PYEH</i>, foot)</dd>
<dt id="mwARg">io</dt><dd id="mwARk">like 'ee-oh': <i id="mwARo">dio</i> (<i id="mwARs">DYOH</i>, gave)</dd>

<dl id="mwASA"><dt id="mwASE">oi, oy</dt><dd id="mwASI">like 'oy' in "boy": <i id="mwASM">soy</i> (<i id="mwASQ">soy</i>, I am)</dd></dl>

<dl id="mwASU"><dt id="mwASY">ua</dt><dd id="mwASc">like 'wa' in "wallet": <i id="mwASg">cuatro</i> (<i id="mwASk">KWAH-troh</i>, four)</dd>
<dt id="mwASo">ue</dt><dd id="mwASs">like 'we' in "well": <i id="mwASw">puedo</i> (<i id="mwAS0">PWEH-doh</i>, I can)</dd>

<dt id="mwATI">uo</dt><dd id="mwATM">like "wo" in "won't": <i id="mwATQ">averiguo</i> (<i id="mwATU">ah-beh-REE-gwoh</i>, I find out)</dd></dl>

</section><section data-mw-section-id="9" id="mwATY" aria-labelledby="Accents_and_stress"><div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Accents_and_stress">Accents and stress</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="//en.wikivoyage.org/w/index.php?title=Spanish_phrasebook&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Accents and stress"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p id="mwATc">Word stress can affect the meaning of the word and generally follows these rules:</p>

<ul id="mwATg"><li id="mwATk"><b id="mwATo">If a word is marked with an accent, then that syllable receives the stress.</b>
<ul id="mwATs"><li id="mwATw">Additionally, if the accent marks a diphthong a syllable break occurs between the two vowels of the diphthong.</li></ul></li></ul>

<ul id="mwAT0"><li id="mwAT4"><b id="mwAT8">If a word is NOT marked with an accent, then</b></li></ul>
<ol id="mwAUA"><li id="mwAUE">if the word ends in a consonant other than <i id="mwAUI">N</i> or <i id="mwAUM">S</i>, the stress occurs on the <b id="mwAUQ">last</b> syllable.</li>
<li id="mwAUU">if the word ends in a vowel, <i id="mwAUY">N</i> or <i id="mwAUc">S</i>, the stress occurs on the <b id="mwAUg">next to last</b> syllable.</li></ol>

<p id="mwAUk">Examples: (1st pronunciation: Iberian; 2nd pronunciation: Latin America; when there is only one, it's shared)</p>
<dl id="mwAUo"><dd id="mwAUs"><b id="mwAUw">círculo</b> (<i id="mwAU0">THEER-koo-loh</i>/<i id="mwAU4">SEER-koo-loh</i>) → circle</dd>
<dd id="mwAU8"><b id="mwAVA">circulo</b> (<i id="mwAVE">theer-KOO-loh</i>/<i id="mwAVI">seer-KOO-loh</i>) → I circulate</dd>
<dd id="mwAVM"><b id="mwAVQ">circuló</b> (<i id="mwAVU">theer-koo-LOH</i>/<i id="mwAVY">seer-koo-LOH</i>) → he/she/it circulated</dd>
<dd id="mwAVc"><b id="mwAVg">estás</b> (<i id="mwAVk">ehs-TAHS</i>) → you are</dd>
<dd id="mwAVo"><b id="mwAVs">estas</b> (<i id="mwAVw">EHS-tahs</i>) → these</dd>
<dd id="mwAV0"><b id="mwAV4">origen</b> (<i id="mwAV8">oh-REE-hehn</i>) → origin</dd>
<dd id="mwAWA"><b id="mwAWE">orígenes</b> (<i id="mwAWI">oh-REE-hehn-ehs</i>) → origins</dd>
<dd id="mwAWM"><b id="mwAWQ">ciudad</b> (<i id="mwAWU">thee-yoo-DAHD</i>/<i id="mwAWY">see-yoo-DAHD</i>) → city</dd>
<dd id="mwAWc"><b id="mwAWg">ciudades</b> (<i id="mwAWk">thee-you-DAH-dehs</i>/<i id="mwAWo">see-yoo-DAH-dehs</i>) → cities</dd></dl>

<p id="mwAWs">An accent can also be used to differentiate between words that are pronounced the same but have different meanings:</p>
<blockquote id="mwAWw">

</section></section><section data-mw-section-id="10" id="mwAYY" aria-labelledby="Castilian_and_Latin_American_Spanish"><div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Castilian_and_Latin_American_Spanish">Castilian and Latin American Spanish</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="//en.wikivoyage.org/w/index.php?title=Spanish_phrasebook&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Castilian and Latin American Spanish"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p id="mwAYc">In addition to pronunciation differences, there are significant lexical differences between the Spanish spoken in Spain and the Spanish spoken in Latin America. There are also often lexical differences between different Latin American Spanish dialects (e.g. pineapple is <i id="mwAYg">piña</i> in Mexico and <i id="mwAYk">ananá</i> in Argentina). In particular, the Spanish spoken in Argentina and Uruguay has strong <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="//en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Italian_phrasebook" title="Italian phrasebook" id="mwAYo">Italian</a> influences, and some items may be better known by their Italian names instead of their standard Spanish names (e.g. <i id="mwAYs">birra</i> instead of <i id="mwAYw">cerveza</i> for beer).</p>

<p id="mwAY0">To overgeneralize, the higher the register of language (think highbrow newspapers or government communiques) the more similar American and Peninsular Spanish will be. However, "slang" and spoken language can diverge significantly, drawing reactions ranging from puerile snickering to baffled looks to simply not being understood when trying to use the wrong slang in the wrong country.</p>

<div style="font-weight:bold;">América</div>

<p>In Spanish, the term <i>América</i> typically refers to the entire American continent from Canada down to Chile, while the United States of America is properly referred to as <i>los Estados Unidos</i>. Saying "<i>Soy americano.</i>" (literally "I am an American.") to mean that you're from the United States is fairly common but may nonetheless earn you a lecture on the meaning of "America", so you should instead say "<i>Soy estadounidense.</i>" (literally "I am a United Statesian").</p>

<p>Be cautious about how you say that you're from a particular state:  A <i>Californio</i> is a person whose family was in California while that land was still part of a Spanish colony, a true <i>Tejano</i> may never have set foot&nbsp;in the modern US state of Texas, and the family of a <i>Neomexicano</i> was speaking Spanish in the 17th and 18th centuries.  Try "Soy de California" (or Texas, or Nuevo México) instead.</p></td></tr>

</section><section data-mw-section-id="14" id="mwApQ" aria-labelledby="Numbers"><div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Numbers">Numbers</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="//en.wikivoyage.org/w/index.php?title=Spanish_phrasebook&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Numbers"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p id="mwApU">In general, the Spanish numbering system is fairly straightforward. For numbers from 21–29, the final "e" of "veinte" is substituted with an "i", and the second digit is added to the back. For numbers from 31–99, the tens and ones are separated by "y" (e.g. 31: treinta y uno; 99: noventa y nueve). The "y" is not used to separate the hundreds from the tens, or the thousands from the hundreds. Numbers from 101 to 199 are named by using <i id="mwApY">ciento</i> + <i id="mwApc">cardinal number</i>. From 200 onwards, the hundreds are named by using the <i id="mwApg">cardinal number</i> + <i id="mwApk">cientos</i> (eg: 200: doscientos), but the numbers 500, 700 and 900 are exceptions to this rule and must be memorised. Naming of the thousands is straightforward, with <i id="mwApo">cardinal number</i> + <i id="mwAps">mil</i>. From the millions onwards, the plural for is used for numbers above 1,000,000. Also, unlike English, Spanish uses the long scale. Therefore, <i id="mwApw">un billón</i> and <i id="mwAp0">un trillón</i> is not the same as the English "one billion" and "one trillion".</p>

<p id="mwAp4">Counting in "hundreds" is unheard of for numbers larger than 1 000. This is also true for years. Instead of "nineteen hundred fifty three" a Spanish speaker would say "mil novecientos cincuenta y tres".</p>

<p id="mwAp8">Opposite to English, a comma "," is used as a decimal point when writing numbers in Spanish, while a period "." is used to separate groups of thousands. This means that the number written 1,000,000.5 in English will usually be written 1.000.000,5 in Spanish (though you may see 1,000,000.5 in Mexico or the U.S.)</p>

<section data-mw-section-id="16" id="mwA1I" aria-labelledby="Clock_time"><div class="mw-heading mw-heading4"><h4 id="Clock_time">Clock time</h4><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="//en.wikivoyage.org/w/index.php?title=Spanish_phrasebook&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Clock time"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p id="mwA1M">When speaking, clock times are normally told using the 12-hour clock. Instead of AM/PM, a clarification can be added: <i id="mwA1Q">de la mañana</i> (morning), <i id="mwA1U">de la tarde</i> (afternoon), <i id="mwA1Y">de la noche</i> (evening/night) or <i id="mwA1c">de la madrugada</i> (late night). Rarely do Spanish speakers use the 24-hour system in conversation. In writing, the practice differs, see <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="#Writing_time_and_date" class="mw-selflink-fragment" id="mwA1g">below</a>.</p>

</section><section data-mw-section-id="21" id="mwA-8" aria-labelledby="Writing_time_and_date"><div class="mw-heading mw-heading4"><h4 id="Writing_time_and_date">Writing time and date</h4><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="//en.wikivoyage.org/w/index.php?title=Spanish_phrasebook&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Writing time and date"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p id="mwA_A">In most Spanish-speaking countries, times are rendered in 24-hour format, with a colon separating hours and minutes, but in Colombia, Venezuela, the Central American countries, and the parts of the USA where Spanish is spoken, times are rendered in 12-hour format, using the colon and AM/PM.</p>

<dl id="mwA_E"><dt id="mwA_I">9 o'clock AM</dt><dd id="mwA_M"><i id="mwA_Q">nueve de la mañana</i> (spoken: <i id="mwA_U">NWEH-beh deh la mah-NYAH-nah</i>), 9:00 (written 24-hour), 9:00 AM (written 12-hour)</dd>
<dt id="mwA_Y"><span typeof="mw:Nowiki" id="mwA_c">12:30 PM</span></dt><dd id="mwA_g"><i id="mwA_k">doce y media de la mañana</i> (spoken: <i id="mwA_o">DOH-seh ee MEH-dyah deh la mah-NYAH-nah</i>), 12:30 (written 24-hour), 12:30 PM (written 12-hour)</dd>
<dt id="mwA_s">1 o'clock PM</dt><dd id="mwA_w"><i id="mwA_0">una de la tarde</i> (spoken: <i id="mwA_4">OOH-nah deh lah TAHR-deh</i>), 13:00 (written 24-hour), 1:00 PM (written 12-hour)</dd>
<dt id="mwA_8">10 o'clock PM</dt><dd id="mwBAA"><i id="mwBAE">diez de la noche</i> (spoken: <i id="mwBAI">dee-EHS deh la NOH-cheh</i>), 22:00 (written 24-hour), 10:00 PM (written 12-hour)</dd>
<dt id="mwBAM">2 o'clock AM</dt><dd id="mwBAQ"><i id="mwBAU">dos de la madrugada</i> or <i id="mwBAY">dos de la mañana</i> (spoken: <i id="mwBAc">DOHS deh la mah-droo-GAH-dah</i> or <i id="mwBAg">DOHS deh la mah-NYAH-nah</i>), 2:00 (written 24-hour), 2:00 AM (written 12-hour)</dd></dl>

<p id="mwBAk">Dates are given in day-month-year form. All spoken and written, long and short forms follow this pattern:</p>

<dl id="mwBAo"><dt id="mwBAs">7 May 2003</dt><dd id="mwBAw">7 de mayo de 2003</dd>
<dt id="mwBA0">23 October 1997</dt><dd id="mwBA4">23 de octubre de 1997</dd></dl>

<p id="mwBA8">Unlike in English numbers of years are always pronounced as normal numbers (i.e. in thousands, not hundreds) thus it is "mil novecientos noventa y dos" ("a thousand nine hundred ninety and two").</p>

<p id="mwBBA">Day–month constructions (<i id="mwBBE">4 de julio</i>, for example) are not usually abbreviated. In the rare cases that an abbreviation is used, the number of the month is not used, but its initial letter is. Usual examples are:</p>

<dl id="mwBBI"><dt id="mwBBM">23-F</dt><dd id="mwBBQ">23 de febrero, date of a failed coup d'état in Spain (1981)</dd>
<dt id="mwBBU">11-S</dt><dd id="mwBBY">11 de septiembre, date of the attack to the Twin Towers (2001) (and of the Chilean coup in 1973).</dd></dl>
